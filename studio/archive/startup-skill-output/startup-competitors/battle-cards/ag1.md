# Battle Card: AG1 (Athletic Greens)

*Updated: 2026-03-12*

---

## Who They Are

AG1 is a New Zealand-founded, US-based single-SKU supplement company ($1.2B valuation, ~$600M estimated annual revenue) selling a daily greens powder at $79-99/month via subscription. Built primarily through podcast sponsorships and influencer marketing (Hugh Jackman, Cindy Crawford), AG1 is the most commercially successful supplement brand in the longevity-adjacent space. However, its scientific credibility is increasingly under fire from Harvard, McGill, ConsumerLab, and high-profile figures like Bryan Johnson.

---

## Strengths (Honest)

- **Revenue scale:** ~$600M annually from a single product — extraordinary. [Estimate]
- **Brand recognition:** Ubiquitous podcast sponsor; near-universal awareness among health-conscious consumers. [Data]
- **Single-SKU simplicity:** Operational efficiency, consistent messaging, no SKU complexity. [Data]
- **Moving to retail (2025):** Expanding offline distribution. [Data]
- **New flavors:** Tropical, Berry, Citrus alongside Original — addressing taste complaints. [Data]
- **Profitable:** Bootstrapped to $150M before raising external capital. [Data]

---

## Weaknesses (Specific)

- **Scientific credibility collapse:** Harvard's Dr. JoAnn Manson criticized lack of "rigor." McGill debunked claims. Only one small company-sponsored trial (30 people, 1 month). No independent research supports marketing claims. [Data]
- **BBB D- rating:** Subscription cancellation complaints, unauthorized charges documented. [Data]
- **Bryan Johnson public rejection:** "Nothing changed when I started taking Athletic Greens" — devastating from the most visible longevity figure. [Data]
- **Lead contamination:** ConsumerLab found lead levels unsafe for children/pregnant women (2022, since reformulated). [Data]
- **Excessive vitamin dosages:** 1,100% daily recommended B7, 467% vitamin C, 50mg B12 (20x recommended). [Data]
- **Proprietary blends hide dosages:** Many ingredients undisclosed in amounts. [Data]
- **No personalization:** Same powder for everyone regardless of individual needs. [Data]
- **Price is #1 complaint:** $79-99/month for a supplement generates sticker shock. [Data]
- **Growing cheaper competition:** Bloom ($35/30 servings), other greens powders targeting younger demographics via TikTok. [Data]
- **CEO controversy:** Founder Ashenden stepped down amid scrutiny of his past in New Zealand (2024). [Data]

---

## How to Win Against Them

1. **Science vs. marketing:** AG1 spent hundreds of millions on influencer marketing but has one small company-funded study. Alche builds on rigorous, multi-dimensional longevity science and lab partnerships. "We test your biology first. AG1 sells the same powder to everyone."

2. **Personalized vs. one-size-fits-all:** AG1 gives everyone the same 75 ingredients. Alche starts with your biomarkers and builds a personalized protocol. "Why take 75 ingredients when you might only need 12?"

3. **Honesty vs. hype:** AG1's claims are disputed by Harvard, McGill, and independent labs. Alche labels every claim with its evidence level and is transparent about what we don't know.

4. **Community vs. product:** AG1 is a powder in a bag. Alche is a cafe, a community, a coaching relationship. "Longevity isn't something you drink once a day."

5. **Value transparency:** AG1 costs $79-99/month for a single supplement with disputed science. Alche's EUR 19/month tier offers more health insight than AG1 provides at 4x the price.

## When They Win Over You (Honest)

- When the customer wants maximum simplicity — one scoop, done, no thinking required.
- When the customer already trusts AG1 from years of podcast exposure.
- When convenience matters more than personalization.
- When the customer doesn't care about (or doesn't know about) the scientific criticism.

## Customer Objections & Responses

**"I've been taking AG1 for years and I feel great."**
"If you feel great, that's what matters. But feeling great and having optimal biomarkers aren't always the same thing. Bryan Johnson felt fine on AG1 too — his bloodwork showed nothing changed. We can actually measure what's happening inside your body, not just how you feel."

**"AG1 replaces 12 supplements in one scoop."**
"If you need all 75 of those ingredients. Most people don't. A $49 blood test can tell you exactly which 5-10 supplements YOUR body actually needs. Why pay $99/month for 65 things you don't need?"

**"But everyone I listen to recommends AG1."**
"AG1 sponsors those podcasts — hosts are paid to recommend it. Harvard, McGill, and independent labs that aren't paid by AG1 tell a different story. We believe in transparency, not sponsorship."

---

## Key Vulnerability to Exploit

**The credibility gap.** AG1's entire business rests on the perception that it's science-backed. Harvard, McGill, ConsumerLab, and Bryan Johnson have each independently challenged this. Every credibility hit compounds. Alche should position as the evidence-based alternative: "We don't ask you to trust our marketing. We show you your own data."
