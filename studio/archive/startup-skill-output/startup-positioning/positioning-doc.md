# Alche Positioning Document

> April Dunford 5+1 framework. Built from Wave 1 (competitive alternatives + customer intelligence) and Wave 2 (market categories + trends). March 2026.

---

## Executive Summary

Alche's positioning must accomplish three things simultaneously: (1) claim defensible ground in a rapidly converging competitive landscape, (2) resonate emotionally with women who are wellness-engaged but optimization-fatigued, and (3) be honest about what Alche IS today (pre-seed, nothing built) vs. what it plans to become.

**The positioning:**

Alche is a **preventive health membership for women** that translates longevity science into daily rituals you actually enjoy — anchored in a physical cafe/apothecary in Berlin, powered by an AI-guided app, and held together by community.

**The one-sentence version:**

For wellness-engaged women in Berlin who want to age well without longevity becoming an obsession, Alche is a membership that combines science-backed guidance, a beautiful physical space, and a community of women on the same path — because health should be a practice you enjoy, not a performance you optimize.

## Component 1: Competitive Alternatives

### What do customers do today instead of Alche?

The target customer — a woman, 30-55, in Berlin, spending EUR 50+/mo on wellness — currently assembles a fragmented stack:

**The typical stack (EUR 120-300/mo total):**
1. Gym or boutique fitness (EUR 40-80/mo)
2. 1-2 wellness apps she checks occasionally (EUR 10-30/mo)
3. Supplements she's not sure about (EUR 30-60/mo)
4. Annual doctor visit + maybe one blood panel (EUR 100-300/yr)
5. Occasional wellness content (Huberman, ZOE podcast, Instagram) — free but overwhelming
6. Peer advice and self-research (time-intensive, anxiety-producing)

**What's wrong with this stack:**
- Nothing talks to anything else
- No longevity framing — it's reactive, not preventive
- No community beyond incidental gym acquaintances
- No personalization — generic advice applied generically
- Total cost is HIGH for fragmented, uncoordinated value

**The specific alternatives and their gaps:**

| Alternative | What it gets right | What it gets wrong |
|------------|-------------------|-------------------|
| ZOE | Science + warmth | Nutrition-only, no physical space, no community |
| Function Health | Deep biomarkers | Cold, clinical, data-without-direction, US-only |
| Noom (longevity) | Behavior change + biomarkers | Terrible trust, no community, weight-loss baggage |
| OURA/WHOOP | Effortless tracking | Data creates anxiety, no guidance, no community |
| ANTI Berlin | Beautiful longevity space | No science depth, no app, no food |
| Doing nothing | No overwhelm | Aging doesn't wait |

No custom hardware. This architecture explicitly avoids each failure mode that killed $800M+ in longevity startups. [Design choice — execution risk remains] |

## Component 4: Best-Fit Customers

### Who cares most about these value themes?

**Primary: The Overwhelmed Seeker**
- Woman, 33-52
- Lives in Berlin (Mitte, Prenzlauer Berg, Kreuzberg, Charlottenburg)
- Spending EUR 50-150/mo on fragmented wellness stack
- In or approaching perimenopause (hormonal changes creating urgency)
- Wellness-engaged: follows health accounts, has tried apps, takes some supplements
- BUT alienated by optimization culture, biohacker aesthetics, metric-obsession
- Craves community around health but hasn't found it
- Has disposable income but is frustrated by paying a lot for disconnected services
- Language: "I want to take care of myself" not "I want to optimize my biology"

**Why she cares MOST:**
- She's already spending the money (just poorly allocated)
- She feels the optimization backlash viscerally (it's personal, not theoretical)
- She wants community (post-pandemic loneliness is real in Berlin's expat/transplant population)
- Perimenopause creates urgency (something is changing, she can feel it)
- The cafe is walking distance (geographic specificity matters)

**Who she is NOT:**
- Not a biohacker (she finds the term off-putting)
- Not a data nerd (she doesn't want another dashboard)
- Not a doctor-shopper (she doesn't want clinical services)
- Not price-insensitive (EUR 99/mo needs to feel like a clear upgrade over her current stack)

**Secondary: The Curious Beginner**
- Woman, 25-35
- Earlier in wellness journey
- Lower willingness to pay (EUR 19-29/mo)
- Attracted by the cafe, the aesthetic, the community
- May convert to full membership over time
- Entry point: cafe visits, community events, app free tier

**Tertiary: The Disillusioned Optimizer**
- Any gender, 30-50
- Tried everything (OURA, WHOOP, protocols, supplements)
- Burned out on tracking
- Ready for "health as a practice" not "health as a project"
- Higher willingness to pay but needs to be convinced the approach works
- May include men who reject biohacker bro culture

## Component 5: Market Category

### Recommended: Preventive Health Membership for Women

**Why this frame:**

1. **"Preventive health"** — Recognized, growing category. Buyers understand it. Links to macro trend of proactive over reactive health. Not intimidating.

2. **"Membership"** — Sets pricing expectations ($20-100/mo). Implies ongoing relationship, not one-off transaction. Signals community.

3. **"For women"** — Clear segmentation. Matches buyer self-identification. Justified by the structural underinvestment (6% of healthcare investment for 51% of population).

**Why not other frames:**

- **"Longevity platform"** → Forces competition with Function Health ($2.5B) and Neko Health ($1.8B). Alche loses on science, data, and resources.
- **"Wellness community"** → Undersells the science and app. Makes it sound like a yoga studio.
- **"Curated longevity lifestyle"** → Category creation. At pre-seed, cannot afford to educate the market.
- **"Femtech"** → Sets clinical expectations Alche can't meet. Investors expect regulatory defensibility.

**The ladder:** Start as "preventive health membership for women in Berlin." Expand to "longevity membership." Eventually, "curated longevity lifestyle brand." But not yet.

## Component 6: Trend Overlay

### Primary Trend: The Over-Optimization Backlash

**The trend (GWS 2026):** "Wellness experiences will embrace what humans actually are: imperfect, emotional, relational and sensory."

**Why this trend strengthens positioning:**
- It's independently validated (GWS, not Alche's framing)
- It creates a clear villain (optimization culture) and hero (joyful practice)
- It's culturally acute in 2026 (orthosomnia, longevity fixation syndrome, Nike shifting to "softness")
- It directly affects buyer evaluation: women choosing Alche are choosing AGAINST optimization culture
- No major brand has claimed this positioning yet

**How to use it:** The trend is the CONTEXT for why Alche exists now. Not "the market is growing" (generic) but "the market is correcting itself, and Alche is the correction."

**Supporting trend:** Women's health going mainstream. This is the structural tailwind that makes the "for women" segmentation not just a niche but a growing, well-funded market.

## Validation Tests

### Neumeier Onliness Test

**Attempt 1:** "Alche is the only preventive health membership that combines longevity science with a physical cafe, community, and joyful positioning — for women, in Europe."

**Assessment:** "Only" is defensible today. Nobody else has physical space + app + science + community + women-first + Europe. But several competitors are converging. The "only" may have a 12-18 month shelf life. [Estimate]

**Attempt 2 (tighter):** "Alche is the only longevity membership where the science comes with a cafe, the community comes with food, and the experience comes with joy."

**Assessment:** More evocative, less precise. Better for marketing, worse for investor clarity.

### Ries/Trout Mental Ladder

**Rung claimed:** "The longevity brand for women who want science without obsession"

**Is it simple enough to remember?** Yes — "longevity for women, science without obsession." Seven words.

**Is the rung available?** YES. Nobody is on it. ZOE is "nutrition science made accessible." Function Health is "know your numbers." AG1 is "one daily habit." Noom is "behavior change." The "science without obsession" rung is empty.

**Can one brand own it?** Yes, if execution matches positioning. The risk is that "science without obsession" attracts people who are neither science-deep nor obsessed — i.e., casual wellness consumers who don't pay premium subscriptions. This MUST be tested. [Opinion]

## Recommendations

### Immediate Actions (Pre-Launch)

1. **Drop "Imperfect Longevity" as the positioning label.** Keep the spirit (joy, anti-optimization) but the word "imperfect" risks attracting the wrong audience — people who want permission to do nothing rather than people who want a better way to do something. Replace with language that conveys both aspiration AND ease.

2. **Build the ZOE slide.** Every investor will ask. Pre-load the answer: "ZOE proved the brand thesis (science + warmth works). We're broader (multi-dimensional), physical (cafe), community-first, and European."

3. **Address Noom's longevity pivot.** This is NEW since Feb 2026 research. When investors ask "what about Noom?", the answer is: "Noom has scale but zero trust (settled $62M billing lawsuit). They added 'longevity' to their weight-loss app. We're building longevity from the ground up with community and physical space — things Noom's model can't replicate."

4. **Frame the 0/6 honestly.** Don't score 6/6 on capabilities that don't exist. Instead: "Here's what exists today: a positioning hypothesis, a team, and a Berlin location. Here's what we'll build with pre-seed capital: cafe (month 1-3), app MVP (month 2-4), first community cohort (month 3-6), first lab partnership (month 4-6). At launch: 3/6. At Series A: 6/6."

5. **Test the positioning with 10 target customers.** Before committing to materials, run concept boards with the "Overwhelmed Seeker" persona in Berlin. Test: Does "preventive health membership" resonate? Does "for women" attract or limit? Does the cafe concept drive interest or skepticism?

### Positioning Risks

| Risk | Severity | Mitigation |
|------|----------|------------|
| "Science without obsession" attracts casual non-payers | HIGH | Pricing gate at EUR 49/mo for core membership. Cafe as low-friction entry. Community events as qualification filter. |
| Competitive convergence closes the window | MEDIUM | Speed. Launch cafe + app in 6 months, not 12. First-mover in Berlin. Physical presence creates local moat. |
| Community hypothesis fails | HIGH | Test with pop-up events before committing to lease. Measure: attendance, social sharing, repeat visits, email signups. |
| "Preventive health membership" sounds boring | LOW | The category frame is for clarity, not excitement. The brand voice, cafe experience, and visual identity carry the excitement. |
| Heilpraktiker bridge carries stigma | MEDIUM | Don't lead with regulatory structure. Lead with experience. Heilpraktiker is an operational detail, not a positioning element. |

## Red Flags
- ALL of Alche's unique attributes are planned, not built. This positioning document describes a future state. The honesty protocol demands this be front and center in any materials.
- The competitive convergence (Noom, WHOOP, Function, Hims, Parsley all adding pieces) is accelerating faster than the prior research suggested. Speed to market is now a critical variable.
- "Imperfect Longevity" as a label risks attracting the wrong customer segment. The positioning spirit is right; the label needs work.

## Yellow Flags
- The EUR 99/mo tier competes in the most contested price band. Differentiation at this price must be visceral and obvious.
- Berlin may not have enough depth in the target segment to sustain a VC-scale business. Munich, Hamburg, and Zurich may be needed sooner than planned.
- The positioning assumes the cafe opens on time. Cafe delays collapse the "physical space" differentiator.
