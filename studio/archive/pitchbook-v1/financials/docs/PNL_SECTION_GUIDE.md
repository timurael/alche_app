**Revenue — 8 Streams + 1 Free Amenity**

This is a product commerce business with a subscription wrapper — retail drives the revenue, subscriptions drive retention and data. Month 24 monthly revenue lands at approximately EUR 264K across all eight streams. Retail products account for ~85% of that; subscriptions are ~5%.

The eight streams are: (1) retail product sales — curated third-party items sold off the shelf; (2) smoothie bar; (3) app subscriptions (EUR 19 / 49 / 99/month, with annual tiers at EUR 190 / 490 / 990); (4) events; (5) doctor SaaS (EUR 99/month per clinic, max 20 clinics); (6) CGM affiliate commissions (EUR 15/user/month, scaling to ~42 users and EUR 630/month by M24); (7) B2B corporate wellness (EUR 585/month from M9, growing to EUR 1,755/month by M18 with 3 clients); (8) gift cards (EUR 441/month net from M4, 10% breakage already applied). LED therapy is a free amenity — no revenue row, zero costs — it exists to increase dwell time and smoothie attachment.

*CGM (Continuous Glucose Monitor) — a wearable sensor that tracks blood sugar in real time. Affiliate commission — alche earns a monthly fee per user referred, without handling hardware. Breakage — the portion of gift cards that are sold but never redeemed; recognised immediately as revenue under German accounting. BGM (Betriebliches Gesundheitsmanagement) — German corporate health management programme; the basis for B2B billing.*

**Cumulative Cash & Runway**

The EUR 500K raise is sufficient but the margin is tighter than the P&L alone suggests. Cash hits its lowest point around M3–M4, when the physical fit-out, key money, and security deposit all land before any revenue has started. The Cash Flow sheet — not the P&L — is the correct place to verify cash never hits zero.

This row accumulates the net profit and loss month-by-month starting from the EUR 500K raise. It is the clearest answer to "is the raise sized correctly?" The trough — the month where cash is lowest — occurs around M3–M4 when CapEx (EUR 148K including EUR 50K key money), Kaution (EUR 7.5K), and pre-revenue operating costs all hit simultaneously. The Cash Flow sheet shows the trough is tighter than the P&L suggests by approximately EUR 218K, because working capital tied up in inventory does not appear as a loss in the P&L but is still real cash leaving the account. Cash must stay above zero in all months — a dip below zero means the raise is under-sized. Verify the exact trough amount in the Cash Flow sheet.

*Runway — how long the company can operate before running out of cash. Working capital — cash tied up in inventory and other operational needs before it converts back to revenue. Kaution — refundable security deposit on the Berlin lease (EUR 7.5K), not a business cost but a real cash outflow. Trough — the lowest point the cumulative cash balance reaches across the 24 months.*

**EBITDA**

The business reaches operational profitability between Month 11 and Month 13. After that point, operations generate more than they cost, and the surplus begins rebuilding the cash balance. By Month 24, EBITDA is solidly positive and trending upward.

EBITDA is the operating profitability signal before accounting for the upfront investment (which is spread over years via depreciation) and before tax. The business is modeled to reach EBITDA break-even between M11 and M13, meaning operations cover their own costs within the first year of trading. Look for the month where EBITDA first turns positive and stays positive — a recovery dip followed by a second crossing would indicate a structural cost issue worth investigating in the Assumptions sheet. By M24, EBITDA should be solidly positive and trending upward as the higher-margin revenue streams (CGM commissions, subscriptions) grow their share.

*EBITDA — Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, and Amortization. In plain terms: does the business make money from its day-to-day operations, before accounting for upfront investments or tax? Break-even — the point where revenue exactly covers costs; every EUR earned after that point is profit.*

**Net P&L (After Tax)**

This is the bottom line after every cost, investment write-off, and tax obligation is subtracted. Early months are deeply negative (no revenue, full setup costs); the path turns positive around M13–M16 and stays there. At Month 24, monthly Net P&L should be positive, demonstrating the business is self-sustaining.

Net P&L is total revenue minus all costs: COGS, payroll, marketing, R&D, admin, depreciation, and the German tax provision. Early months show large negative figures (M1–M3 are pre-revenue with full setup costs hitting simultaneously). The trajectory from deeply negative to positive is the core story investors read here. At M24, Net P&L should be positive on a monthly basis, demonstrating the business can sustain itself without further external funding. Look for any unexpected dips after M13 — smooth recovery is the signal that costs are well-controlled.

*P&L (Profit and Loss) — the income statement; a month-by-month record of what was earned and what was spent. After tax — the final number after German corporate tax has been deducted.*

**Gross Profit & Gross Margin %**

Gross margin starts at 45–55% in the first operating months and improves toward ~68% as the business matures. This trend is healthy — it means the product mix is shifting toward higher-margin revenue. A gross margin below 40% in any post-Month 4 month is a red flag.

Gross profit is revenue minus the direct cost of delivering each product or service, before fixed costs (payroll, rent, marketing) are subtracted. Gross margin percentage is what fraction of each EUR of revenue the business keeps after covering delivery costs. It starts in the 45–55% range in early operating months and improves toward ~68% in later months as CGM commissions and subscriptions — both near-100% gross margin — grow their share of the mix. The key question here is whether margin trends upward month-over-month; a flat or declining gross margin would signal that the higher-COGS streams (retail, smoothies) are growing faster than planned.

*Gross margin — what percentage of revenue remains after the direct cost of making or delivering the product. E.g., 55% gross margin on EUR 100 of sales means EUR 55 left to cover rent, staff, and other fixed costs. COGS (Cost of Goods Sold) — the direct cost to produce or deliver each unit sold.*

**Key Metrics Block**

The summary block at the bottom of the P&L sheet surfaces the numbers investors ask for first: LTV:CAC, ARPU, burn rate, and break-even flags. Subscription ARPU is EUR 38.44 — not EUR 49 — because most subscribers are on the EUR 19 Essential tier. Monthly burn in M1–M3 is the most closely watched figure.

This block surfaces LTV:CAC ratio, ARPU, monthly burn rate, and break-even flags in a scannable format without requiring the reader to build the calculation themselves. Subscription ARPU is EUR 38.44 — not EUR 49 — because the subscriber base is weighted toward the EUR 19 Essential tier in the early months, with Premium (EUR 49) and Elite (EUR 99) growing over time. Monthly burn is the most investor-critical number in M1–M3; it should decline as a percentage of the EUR 500K raise each month, confirming runway extends to break-even. The break-even flag turns green when EBITDA first crosses zero and should not revert.

*LTV:CAC — Lifetime Value to Customer Acquisition Cost ratio. How much a customer is worth over their lifetime vs. how much it cost to acquire them. A ratio above 3:1 is generally considered healthy. ARPU — Average Revenue Per User; the average monthly subscription income per subscriber. Burn rate — how many EUR the company spends each month in excess of what it earns.*

**Cash Flow Sheet**

The Cash Flow sheet shows EUR 218K less available cash than the P&L implies, because inventory purchases are a real cash outflow that the P&L does not record as a cost until the goods are sold. The Kaution (EUR 7.5K deposit) also appears only here. This is the sheet to trust for "do we have enough cash?"

The Cash Flow sheet closes the gap between P&L profit and actual bank balance. The primary driver of the gap is inventory: physical retail products must be bought before they can be sold, so the business always holds working capital in stock. As revenue scales, inventory build accelerates, increasing the cash required. The Kaution (EUR 7.5K refundable security deposit) appears here as a cash outflow in M3 but does not appear in the P&L because it is refundable at lease end and is not a business cost. When evaluating whether EUR 500K is sufficient, use the Cash Flow sheet — not the P&L cumulative row — to find the true cash trough.

*Cash Flow — tracks the actual movement of money in and out of the bank account, including items the P&L does not record (like prepaid inventory). Kaution — German term for a refundable security deposit; legally distinct from an expense. Working capital — cash tied up in inventory, receivables, and other operational needs before it converts back into revenue.*

**COGS**

Retail products carry ~48% COGS; smoothies ~35%; subscriptions and CGM commissions near zero. As higher-margin streams grow, blended COGS compresses from ~45–55% of revenue in early months toward ~32% by maturity. A rising COGS percentage in later months would signal unexpected volume in low-margin streams.

Each revenue stream has its own cost-to-deliver rate. Retail products are curated third-party items sold off the shelf — wholesale cost gives roughly ~48% COGS and ~52% gross margin; prices vary by product. Smoothies: ~35% COGS (~65% margin). Subscriptions: near-zero marginal cost after infrastructure. Events: ~40% COGS in catering and venue. CGM commissions: pure affiliate, zero COGS, EUR 15 per active user per month. The blended COGS line should decline as a percentage of revenue over time, reflecting the growing share of high-margin streams. Total COGS consuming more than 55% of revenue in any post-M6 month is worth investigating.

*COGS (Cost of Goods Sold) — the direct cost to make or deliver one unit of a product or service. For a smoothie: ingredients and labor. For a subscription: server costs. Blended COGS — the average cost rate across all revenue streams combined, weighted by volume.*

**B2B Corporate Wellness**

This stream activates at Month 9 with the first corporate client at EUR 585/month, grows to two clients (EUR 1,170/month) by M12, and three clients (EUR 1,755/month) by M18. It is a high-margin, low-COGS stream — no additional staff or physical cost per client. The billing model is EUR 39 per employee per month under the BGM framework (German corporate health management).

The B2B stream targets Berlin tech companies offering wellness stipends to employees — a common benefit in the local market. Billing is structured as a software/service fee (EUR 39/PEPM) rather than a per-session or per-product charge, making it a recurring contract revenue line with near-zero incremental cost. Zero COGS: no physical product is shipped and no additional staff are required per client. The M24 contribution from three clients is EUR 1,755/month — small relative to total revenue but high-margin and contractual.

*BGM (Betriebliches Gesundheitsmanagement) — German statutory framework for corporate employee health programmes; companies receive tax incentives for spending on certified wellness providers. PEPM (Per Employee Per Month) — standard B2B SaaS pricing unit in corporate wellness; EUR 39/PEPM means alche bills EUR 39 per enrolled employee per month.*

**Gift Cards**

Gift cards generate EUR 441/month net revenue from Month 4 onward, with 10% breakage already applied. They are a zero-COGS revenue line — cash is collected upfront and revenue is recognised either on redemption or at breakage. At EUR 441/month they are a modest but reliable contribution from M4.

The gross gift card revenue is approximately EUR 490/month (10 cards × EUR 49 average value); the 10% breakage deduction (EUR 49) reduces the recognised revenue to EUR 441/month net. Breakage represents cards sold but never redeemed — under German accounting (HGB), this is recognised as revenue at the point it becomes statistically clear the card will not be used. Gift cards also serve a marketing function: they drive new customer acquisition at zero marginal cost and typically convert to higher-value subsequent visits.

*Breakage — the portion of gift cards sold but never redeemed; recognised as revenue under German accounting once redemption is statistically unlikely. Zero COGS — gift cards have no direct cost to deliver; all EUR 441 flows to gross profit.*

**Scenarios**

Three versions of the model run in parallel: Base (as-built), Bull (1.3× revenue growth), and Bear (0.6× revenue growth). The Bear case is what matters for the investment decision — if cash stays positive there, the EUR 500K raise is sized correctly. All cost assumptions remain fixed across scenarios, so the Bear case tests whether fixed costs are manageable at lower-than-expected revenue.

The Bear case applies a 0.6× multiplier to revenue growth rates while leaving all cost lines unchanged — payroll, compliance, CapEx, and admin stay at base-case levels regardless of which scenario is active. This is the relevant stress test: can the business survive with 40% less revenue growth than modeled? Under Bull (1.3× growth), EBITDA break-even arrives earlier and M24 surplus is larger, but the Bull case should not be used to justify the raise size. The Base and Bear cases are the investment decision inputs.

*Scenario analysis — running the same model with different assumptions to test best-case, expected, and worst-case outcomes. Bull case — optimistic; assumes faster growth than expected. Bear case — pessimistic; assumes slower growth. The bear case is the safety check.*

Founder salaries for Timu and Daria are not included at pre-seed stage. Payroll as a percentage of revenue should decline steadily from M4 onward as revenue scales faster than headcount.

Operational staff (smoothie bar, front-of-house) are added at M4 when the physical space opens. *Pre-seed — the earliest stage of startup funding, before product-market fit is proven. Headcount — total number of paid staff. Recruitment fee — the one-time fee paid to a staffing agency to find and place a candidate, typically 15–20% of annual salary.*

**Sales & Marketing**

This section captures what the business spends to acquire customers: paid digital advertising, influencer partnerships, content production, and brand events. CAC should produce a payback period under 12 months. Spend steps up at defined growth inflection points; each step-up is documented in the Decisions sheet.

Aggregate monthly S&M spend reflects the total cost of acquiring both walk-in customers and app subscribers. Customer acquisition cost (CAC) for subscribers is calculated in the Unit Economics sheet; the P&L shows the total spending envelope. Marketing spend increases at specific growth phase transitions — these appear as visible spikes in the monthly rows and are explained in the Decisions sheet (D-series entries). The ratio of S&M spend to new subscriber additions is the metric to track here: if CAC rises while the payback period extends beyond 12 months, the marketing strategy needs revision.

*CAC (Customer Acquisition Cost) — the total marketing and sales spend divided by the number of new customers acquired. If you spend EUR 1,000 on ads and gain 10 new subscribers, CAC is EUR 100. CAC payback period — how many months of subscription revenue are needed to recover the cost of acquiring that subscriber.*

**CapEx**

Total upfront capital investment is ~EUR 148K, concentrated in M2–M3. The largest single item is the EUR 50K Ablöse — a key money payment to the outgoing tenant, required to take over the Berlin lease. Unlike the EUR 7.5K Kaution (deposit), CapEx is a permanent cost that gets written off over time through the P&L.

CapEx covers the investment required before the space can open: physical fit-out and construction, LED therapy panels (EUR 5K), tech hardware, and the EUR 50K Ablöse (key money paid to the outgoing occupant as compensation for fixtures and goodwill — standard in Berlin commercial leases). This is a one-time outflow concentrated in M2–M3. The Kaution (EUR 7.5K refundable security deposit) is often confused with CapEx but is not — it is a cash outflow that appears only in the Cash Flow sheet because it is refundable at lease end. The EUR 50K Ablöse is treated as an intangible asset (leasehold premium) and amortized over the lease term rather than expensed in full in M3.

*CapEx (Capital Expenditure) — money spent on physical or long-term assets (equipment, fit-out) rather than day-to-day operations. Unlike monthly costs, CapEx is spread across the P&L over years via depreciation. Ablöse — German term for key money; a one-time payment to the outgoing occupant compensating them for fixtures, goodwill, and improvements they leave behind. Amortized — spread out as a cost over multiple years rather than recognized as a lump sum.*

**Depreciation & EBIT**

The ~EUR 148K CapEx is spread as a cost across 3–5 years rather than hitting the P&L all at once. EBIT shows profitability after this write-off is applied. EBIT will always be lower than EBITDA, but should track it in direction.

Depreciation allocates the cost of the physical investment over the useful life of the assets. Fit-out and hardware depreciate over 3–5 years under German GAAP. EBIT (EBITDA minus depreciation and amortization) shows what profit looks like after acknowledging that the physical investment is being consumed over time. The EUR 50K Ablöse is amortized over the lease term as an intangible asset. EBIT being lower than EBITDA is expected and healthy — the gap represents the structured write-off of the upfront investment, not new cash leaving the business.

*Depreciation — the annual accounting cost of a physical asset wearing out or aging. A EUR 50K fit-out depreciated over 5 years adds EUR 10K/year as a P&L cost, even though the cash was spent upfront. EBIT — Earnings Before Interest and Taxes; EBITDA minus depreciation. German GAAP — the accounting rules companies in Germany must follow (Handelsgesetzbuch / HGB).*

**Tax Provision**

Germany applies a combined ~30% corporate tax rate. Critically, no tax is owed until the company's cumulative profit turns positive — early monthly profits are sheltered by the accumulated losses from the pre-revenue phase. The first tax provision appears around M13–M16 depending on scenario.

The model applies a ~30% combined rate: Körperschaftsteuer (~15%) plus solidarity surcharge plus Berlin Gewerbesteuer (~14–17%). In Germany, a GmbH does not owe profit tax until cumulative profits across all periods turn positive — losses carried forward from M1–M12 offset early gains. This means no tax rows appear in the early months even if a single month shows positive EBITDA, because the accumulated operating losses absorb those gains first. The first tax provision row should appear only once cumulative profits cross zero, which the model places around M13–M16 depending on which scenario is active.

*GmbH (Gesellschaft mit beschränkter Haftung) — the German equivalent of a limited liability company. Körperschaftsteuer — German corporate income tax (~15%). Gewerbesteuer — German trade tax levied by the municipality; Berlin rate is ~14–17%. Cumulative profit — total profit across all months combined, not just the current month's result.*

**General & Admin**

G&A carries the German-specific compliance costs that most pre-seed models skip entirely. Each line traces to a specific regulation. G&A is low in M1–M3 (pre-launch) and steps up materially at M4 when the physical space activates all compliance obligations simultaneously.

G&A includes: Gewerbesteuer (trade tax), Nebenkosten (building operating costs: heat, electricity, cleaning), GEMA (music licensing), HACCP compliance (mandatory food safety documentation), DSB data protection officer fee (legally required for health data processors), Rundfunkbeitrag (broadcasting contribution), KSK levy on creative freelancer payments, and Verpackungsgesetz (packaging registry for retail products). None of these are estimates — each traces to a specific German regulatory source. Most early-stage models in Germany omit all of them, which creates misleading burn rates. The step-up at M4 is sharp; the month-over-month G&A increase at M4 should be visible clearly in the rows.

*Nebenkosten — German term for ancillary operating costs included in a commercial lease (utilities, building maintenance). GEMA — German music rights collecting society; any business playing music publicly must pay a monthly fee. HACCP — Hazard Analysis and Critical Control Points; mandatory food safety compliance documentation for any business handling food. DSB — Datenschutzbeauftragter (Data Protection Officer); legally required when processing sensitive health data. KSK — Künstlersozialkasse; German social security levy applied when a company pays creative freelancers.*

**R&D**

R&D covers the fixed monthly cost of keeping the app running and improving: servers, health data APIs, AI personalization, and development sprints. These costs are largely flat in EUR terms regardless of user count. As revenue scales, R&D as a percentage of revenue compresses significantly — this is the software leverage point.

R&D line items include: server and cloud infrastructure, third-party health data API licensing (Apple Health, Google Fit integrations), AI personalization model API costs (per-token, variable but small), and ongoing development capacity. These costs are mostly fixed per month rather than scaling with user count, which means R&D efficiency improves automatically as revenue grows. The number to watch is AI API cost per active user — if users engage with personalization features more than modeled, this line will exceed budget without changing headcount. R&D as a share of revenue should fall from a high early percentage toward a stable low single-digit share by M24.

*API (Application Programming Interface) — a connection that allows one software system to pull data from another; e.g., alche pulling sleep data from Apple Health. Per-token cost — AI models charge per unit of text processed; costs are small per interaction but accumulate with scale. Fixed cost — a cost that does not change based on how many customers you have.*

**Management & APIs**

This section covers the SaaS subscriptions and infrastructure costs the business runs on: PMS/POS software, health API licensing, payment processing fees, and third-party data subscriptions. Costs here are a mix of flat monthly fees and small variable costs that scale loosely with transaction volume. At Month 24, this line should represent a low single-digit percentage of revenue.

PMS/POS software (property management and point-of-sale), health API licensing, AI model API costs, payment gateway processing fees, and any third-party wellness data subscriptions all sit here. These are largely flat monthly SaaS fees with some variable component (payment processing fees scale with transaction volume). With ~339 subscribers and ~EUR 264K monthly revenue at M24, this line should remain a minor overhead item. The exception to watch: if AI personalization usage per user grows materially faster than modeled, per-token API costs will exceed budget and should trigger a review of the AI cost assumptions in the Assumptions sheet.

*PMS — Property Management System; software managing bookings, memberships, and space scheduling. POS — Point of Sale; the checkout and payment system. Payment processing fee — the percentage charged by payment processors (Stripe, etc.) on every transaction, typically 1.4–2.9%. SaaS — Software as a Service; monthly software subscription rather than a one-time purchase.*

**Unit Economics Sheet**

This sheet isolates profitability per customer and per revenue stream. Subscription ARPU is EUR 38.44 — not EUR 49 — due to tier mix weighting toward EUR 19. At Month 24, subscriptions are only ~4.7% of total revenue despite 339 paying members.

The Unit Economics sheet shows LTV:CAC ratio, CAC payback period in months, contribution margin by revenue stream, and blended subscription ARPU broken out by tier. The ARPU figure (EUR 38.44) reflects actual tier distribution — weighted toward the EUR 19 Essential tier in early months, with Premium and Elite growing over time — not the midpoint tier price. The most important M24 data point: subscriptions represent ~4.7% of total revenue despite 339 paying members. This confirms the model thesis: subscriptions generate retention and data, but physical retail (85% of revenue) is what makes the economics work at this stage.

*LTV (Lifetime Value) — total revenue expected from one customer across their entire relationship with the business. LTV:CAC ratio — LTV divided by the cost to acquire that customer; above 3:1 is healthy. Contribution margin — revenue minus direct variable costs for a specific stream; what that stream contributes toward covering fixed costs. ARPU — Average Revenue Per User (monthly).*

**Assumptions Sheet**

All ~143 input cells (v4) that drive every calculation in the model live here. Changing any single cell recalculates the entire P&L, Cash Flow, and Unit Economics sheets simultaneously. This is the correct place for sensitivity analysis — do not edit P&L rows directly.

Input categories include: pricing across all six revenue streams, monthly growth rates by phase (pre-launch / early traction / growth / scaling), COGS rates per stream, payroll schedule and headcount timing, German compliance costs (Gewerbesteuer rate, GEMA, DSB, KSK fees), CapEx line items, monthly churn rate (locked at 8%), CGM commission rate (EUR 15/user/month), and the subscription tier mix at each phase. Every assumption has a source reference. The Assumptions sheet is the correct interface for scenario testing — if an investor wants to change growth rates or pricing, this is where to do it, not in the P&L rows.

*Sensitivity analysis — changing one assumption at a time to see how much it moves the final result. Churn rate — the percentage of subscribers who cancel each month; 8% means roughly 1 in 12 subscribers leaves per month. Tier mix — the distribution of subscribers across the EUR 19, EUR 49, and EUR 99 plans.*

**Growth Curves Sheet**

This sheet defines unit volume: how many products sell, smoothies are served, events run, and CGM devices activate each month. The subscriber funnel also lives here — new subscribers per month, by tier, by growth phase. Edit this sheet to stress-test subscriber acquisition and churn scenarios.

Monthly revenue is driven by unit volumes defined here, not by growth percentages alone. The subscriber model applies the 8% monthly churn rate to the existing base each period and adds new subscribers, producing the net subscriber count that flows into the P&L. Growth curves are defined by phase: pre-launch (M1–M3), early traction (M4–M9), growth (M10–M18), and scaling (M19–M24). At M24, base-case growth curves produce ~339 total subscribers and approximately 4,600 retail product units sold per month. v4 added two further input rows: row 21 (CGM Active Users — monthly ramp of eligible CGM users whose count feeds the CGM commission formula) and row 22 (B2B Clients — the 1→2→3 client ramp that, multiplied by Assumptions!B133 × B134, drives B2B corporate revenue). Changing subscriber acquisition rates here while leaving churn fixed is the fastest way to test whether the subscription base is sustainable.

*Subscriber funnel — the pipeline of new subscribers entering each month minus those who cancel. Net subscriber count — total active paying subscribers after new additions and cancellations are applied. Unit volume — the raw count of physical items sold or services delivered per period.*

**Decisions Sheet**

Every modeling judgment call (D1–D11) and all seven expert persona audit findings (P1–P7) are documented here. This sheet exists to show the model has been stress-tested, not just built. Any reviewer who questions a specific assumption can trace the rationale directly here.

Documented decisions include: treating the Ablöse as an intangible asset (D-series); switching the doctor revenue model from illegal commission-based to SaaS (legal finding from the compliance lawyer persona); setting churn at 8% (industry benchmark source); excluding founder salaries at pre-seed stage (standard Berlin practice, HR persona confirmed); and CGM access gating behind a 3-month Premium tier hold. The seven expert personas are: angel investor, German tax advisor, operations manager, food safety inspector, growth CFO, compliance lawyer, and HR manager — each reviewed the model and their findings were incorporated into the version documented here. This sheet demonstrates the model has been revised in response to challenge, not built once and left unchanged.

*Audit trail — a documented record of every decision and change, with the rationale. Expert persona review — a structured process where the model is evaluated from the perspective of a specific professional (lawyer, tax advisor, etc.) to identify gaps or errors before investors see it.*
