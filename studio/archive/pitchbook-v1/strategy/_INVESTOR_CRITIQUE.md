# Investor Critique: alche Pitch Book

**Reviewer profile:** European angel investor, EUR 2M+ deployed across 15 health-tech and consumer wellness startups, 300+ pitch books reviewed, deep DACH market knowledge.

**Date reviewed:** February 2026

**Verdict:** Conditional interest. I would take the meeting. I would not wire money today.

---

## 1. First Impression (Cover + Sections 00-02)

**Does this make me want to keep reading?** Yes -- but cautiously.

The cover is the strongest first impression I have seen from a pre-seed pitch book. "Pre-Seed Investment Memorandum" signals seriousness. The three pillars (Taste, Trust, Cultural Authority) are unusual and provocative -- most founders lead with "AI-powered" or "data-driven" and I glaze over. This made me curious about what category they are actually creating.

Section 01 (The Insight) hits hard. The "77% churn by Day 3" stat and the "fragmented stack" visual showing EUR 126-295/mo in disconnected spend -- this is the kind of problem framing that makes me lean forward. The line "They are not lost users. They are our addressable market" is exactly how a good founder frames an opportunity.

Section 02 (Who We Serve) -- the Mira persona is well-constructed. "Wore an Oura ring for five months -- checked her sleep score every morning until it started giving her anxiety about not sleeping well enough. The ring is on her nightstand." I know this person. I have met her at events in Berlin. This feels researched from real conversations, not fabricated in a strategy deck.

The "rebundling opportunity" (EUR 126-295 fragmented into EUR 49-99 integrated) is a compelling visual argument. I immediately understand the economic thesis.

**What gave me pause:** The cover says "The Art of Curated Longevity" which is beautiful branding but immediately triggered my "is this a brand exercise or a business?" filter. That tension runs through the entire book.

## 2. Credibility Check

**Do the founders seem like they know what they are doing?**

The research depth is genuinely impressive. 35+ research sessions, 30+ companies analyzed, $1.4B in failures studied -- and crucially, the data points are sourced. Most pre-seed pitch books I see cite "market research" without naming a single source. This book names OECD Health at a Glance 2025, Destatis, GWI 2024, SpringerMedizin, BEUC, RevenueCat -- that is Series A-level citation discipline from a pre-product company.

The competitive analysis is the best I have seen at pre-seed. The 2x2 positioning map, the specific data on each competitor (Oura $11B, ZOE ~$100M rev, AG1 $600M), and the graveyard analysis (Forward burning $657M on hardware pods, DayTwo with 2,089 citations dying when GLP-1 arrived) -- this is not surface-level Googling. These founders have genuinely studied why companies in this space fail.

**Where it feels "researched but not built":**

This is the book's central tension. Everything from Section 05 onward describes a product that does not exist yet. The KDGB flywheel (Know, Do, Get, Belong) is intellectually elegant but entirely theoretical. The four-tier pricing model, the conversion funnel, the unit economics -- all modeled, none validated.

Specific passages that trigger my "theory, not practice" alarm:

- **Section 06 (Product), page 12:** "Six signature Alche Potions (EUR 7-11) with five functional boost add-ons (+EUR 2 each), mapped to outcome categories. Pre-ordered during booking, ready on arrival. Average ticket: EUR 11." This is extremely specific for something that has never been sold to a single customer. How do they know the average ticket is EUR 11? That is a modeled number, not a measured one.

- **Section 07 (Business Model), page 14:** "5-15x LTV:CAC" -- that range is so wide it communicates uncertainty rather than confidence. A 5x LTV:CAC is decent. A 15x LTV:CAC is exceptional. Putting both in the same card means they genuinely do not know, which is honest, but the presentation makes it look like a confident claim.

- **Section 09 (Traction), page 19:** The entire traction section is about research depth and strategic assets, not market traction. "35+ research sessions" is not traction. "300 newsletter subscribers" would be traction. "10 pre-orders" would be traction. I understand this is pre-seed, but the section title promises something it does not deliver.

## 3. The 5 Questions I Would Ask in the Meeting

**Question 1: What have you actually sold?**
The book describes a sophisticated multi-layered platform. But has a single person paid you any amount of money for anything? A EUR 15 event ticket? A consulting call? A Notion template with longevity protocols? I need to see that someone has exchanged euros for something you made. Zero revenue is fine at pre-seed. Zero transactions is a yellow flag.

**Question 2: Who specifically is the CTO candidate, and why have they not committed?**
Section 11 says "A technical lead candidate has been identified and the 16-week MVP has been scoped with them." If you have scoped a 16-week sprint with someone, that is a substantial relationship. Why are they not on the cap table with a cliff? Is it purely a funding question, or are there other reservations? If this person walks, what is Plan B? The entire execution plan depends on this hire.

**Question 3: How do you plan to get to 2,000 free users by Month 12?**
The conversion funnel on page 15 starts with 2,000 free users converting at 4.5% to 90 paid Core subscribers. Where do 2,000 free users come from in a niche wellness app in Berlin with zero marketing spend? The content-first GTM strategy on Section 08 shows Q4 targets of 15-20K social followers and 5,000 newsletter subscribers -- but the connection between "follows us on TikTok" and "downloads the app and creates an account" is not established. What is the funnel from content consumer to app user?

**Question 4: What happens if the physical space does not work?**
The model assumes EUR 10K/month in space revenue as part of the break-even equation. But the space does not exist yet. The search has not even begun (Section 08, Q4 roadmap: "Physical space search begins"). What if Berlin commercial rents in Mitte are prohibitive? What if the space economics do not work? Is there a digital-only path to break-even, and if so, what does it look like?

**Question 5: Why you and not a well-funded US player entering Europe?**
The book claims a "12-18 month window" before US incumbents integrate European longevity protocols. But Hims already filed for EU operations. Oura has retail presence across DACH. ZOE is UK-based and one localization away from Germany. If ZOE raised another $100M and launched in Germany with Tim Spector's credibility, what is alche's defensible position with EUR 500K and no product?

## 4. Weak Sections

### 4a. Section 09 (Traction) -- `section-09-traction.html`

**The weakest section in the book.**

The title promises "What we have done with zero capital" but the content is: we researched a lot, we built a brand identity, and we have a medical advisor. The "signal grid" shows "35+ research sessions / 30+ companies analyzed / $1.4B in failures studied" -- this is input metrics, not output metrics.

**Specific passage, page 19-20:**
> "Pre-seed means pre-product. What matters is proof of thinking, proof of market understanding, and proof that the founders can execute before a single euro arrives."

This is defensive framing. The book is trying to redefine what traction means rather than demonstrating the standard definition. At pre-seed, I expect at minimum: a waitlist with real signups, social media presence with engagement metrics, LOIs from potential partners, or documented customer discovery interviews with quotes.

The "Content Engine" section on page 20 says: "The first content pieces are being produced: a collagen deep dive across 23 peer-reviewed studies." Being produced is not produced. Show me the published piece. Show me engagement metrics. Show me a screenshot of comments from real people saying "I would pay for this."

**Suggested fix:** Replace the research metrics with actual market signals. Even small ones. How many people responded to a Instagram poll? How many DMs did you get asking about the product after a meetup? Did the medical advisor's patients express interest? Convert the "What exists today" list from a planning document into an evidence document. If the content is actually being produced, publish it before the pitch book goes out and include real engagement data.

### 4b. Section 10 (The Moat) -- `section-10-moat.html`

**Honest but unconvincing.**

The opening line is commendable: "We do not claim a moat. We have a thesis about what compounds." That is refreshingly honest. But then the section describes three moat phases (Social Identity, Network Effects, Switching Costs) that are entirely theoretical.

**Specific passage, page 21:**
> "'Being in my Alche era' becomes part of how people talk about themselves."

This is aspirational brand copy, not a moat analysis. Nobody is saying "my Alche era" because the product does not exist. Comparing to CrossFit and Peloton at peak is reaching -- those brands took years and hundreds of millions of dollars to build identity-level attachment.

**Suggested fix:** Remove the CrossFit/Peloton/Soho House comparisons. They are indefensible at this stage. Instead, frame the moat section around the structural advantages of the business model: the integration thesis (which is actually compelling), the GDPR-native design in a market where 92% of consumers refuse to share health data, and the physical-digital hybrid as a retention mechanism that pure-digital competitors cannot replicate. Those are structural arguments, not brand aspiration.

### 4c. Section 07 (Business Model), Conversion Funnel -- `section-07-business-model.html`

**Specific passage, page 15:**
> "2,000 free users -> 4.5% convert to paid -> ~90 Core subscribers"

The 4.5% free-to-paid conversion rate is cited without justification. RevenueCat 2025 benchmarks for health/fitness apps show ~2-3% trial-to-paid conversion, and that is from people who already downloaded the app and started a trial. A 4.5% rate from free community member to paid subscriber is aggressive. Where does this number come from?

Similarly, the "~30-40 direct-to-paid from physical space" assumption is completely unanchored. How many people walk into the physical space per month? What percentage convert? These are numbers pulled from nowhere.

**Suggested fix:** Show the math. State the assumptions explicitly: "Assuming X walk-ins per month, Y% try a session, Z% of those convert to a subscription based on [comparable data]." If you are going to claim 4.5% free-to-paid, cite the benchmark that supports it. If the honest answer is "we modeled a range and picked the middle," say that.

### 4d. Section 03 (Market), TAM/SAM/SOM -- `section-03-market.html`

**The TAM is lazy.**

**Specific passage, page 6:**
> "TAM: $6.3T -- Global wellness economy (GWI 2024). Context marker -- not a revenue claim."

I appreciate the "context marker" disclaimer, but citing the entire global wellness economy as your TAM is the pitch book equivalent of saying your restaurant's TAM is "the food industry." Every pre-seed wellness startup cites this number. It tells me nothing.

The SAM (EUR 3.2-4.1B for Germany) is better but still lumps together supplements, functional beverages, digital health apps, and premium wellness memberships -- categories with very different dynamics.

The SOM is actually good: "300 members Year 1 conservative, 600 base case." That is grounded and realistic. Lead with that.

**Suggested fix:** Delete or de-emphasize the TAM. Lead with the SOM and work upward. "We need 145 paying subscribers to break even. There are 108K 30-44 year olds in Mitte alone. Here is why capturing 0.1% of that population is achievable." That is a much more compelling market argument than $6.3T.

### 4e. Section 13 (Closing) -- `section-13-closing.html`

**Specific passage, page 25:**
> "Phase 4 // M18-36: Platform maturity. Open API for partner integrations. Corporate wellness tier. 'Alche Certified' partner spaces across EU."

This is vision-deck territory for a company that has not built an MVP. "Alche Certified partner spaces across EU" when you have zero paying customers and no physical space? This undermines the credibility the earlier sections carefully built. At pre-seed, the investor does not want to hear about your European certification program. They want to know you can get 50 people in Berlin to pay you EUR 19/month.

**Suggested fix:** Cut Phase 4 entirely or reduce it to a single sentence: "Beyond Month 18, the model is designed to expand digitally across EU markets." Do not oversell the vision when the execution gap is this large.

## 5. Strong Sections

### 5a. Section 04 (Competitive Landscape) -- Best section in the book.

The 2x2 positioning map is clear and well-placed. The graveyard analysis ("What $1.4B Taught Us") with the three survivor patterns (Asset-Light, Content-Led, Community-First) is the "oh, that is smart" moment. This demonstrates genuine strategic thinking. The three rules are not generic platitudes; they are derived from specific company failures (Forward's $657M hardware burn, DayTwo's 2,089 citations that did not save it from GLP-1 disruption).

The competitive window framing ("12-18 months") with the supporting evidence that Oura has not localized beyond hardware retail, Hims EU is nascent, and ZOE is nutrition-only -- this is the section that would make me schedule the follow-up call.

### 5b. Section 01 (The Insight) -- The Longevity Paradox framing.

"People became project managers of their own health" is a genuinely resonant insight. I have heard this exact frustration in conversations with founders of portfolio companies. The fragmented stack visual (8 disconnected services totaling EUR 126-295/mo) makes the problem visceral and concrete. This is not abstract market analysis -- it is a problem description that investors and customers both immediately recognize.

### 5c. Section 02 (Who We Serve) -- The Mira persona.

This is the best persona construction I have seen in a pitch book. The Oura ring detail, the Levels cancellation, the six half-empty supplement bottles -- these are not invented. Either the founders know this person intimately (because they ARE this person) or they have done exceptional customer discovery. The "rebundling opportunity" from EUR 126-295 fragmented to EUR 49-99 integrated is a clean economic argument.

### 5d. Section 05 (The Solution) -- KDGB Flywheel.

The KNOW-DO-GET-BELONG framework is intellectually elegant and operationally specific. "Each step creates demand for the next" is a genuine flywheel dynamic, not a buzzword. The observation that "you cannot personalize a protocol without data, and you cannot sustain a protocol without community" is the kind of structural insight that separates thinkers from pitch-deck-generators.

### 5e. Section 12 (The Ask) -- Clean and professional.

Wandeldarlehen at EUR 2.5M cap with 20% discount is standard and fair for German pre-seed. The break-even math is transparent (145 paying subscribers + EUR 10K/mo space). The "Even at EUR 400K raise, the model reaches break-even with a EUR 42K buffer" demonstrates scenario modeling. The decision to omit detailed use-of-funds and mark it "[Detailed allocation shared separately upon request]" is the right call -- it creates a reason for the follow-up meeting without looking evasive.

## 6. Deal-Breaker Scan

**No immediate deal-breakers, but three orange flags:**

1. **No CTO and no product.** The entire investment thesis depends on a person who has not committed and a product that does not exist. I have seen this pattern fail many times. The CTO candidate may demand different terms, may not be the right fit, or may not exist in the way described. If the first three months after funding are consumed by a CTO search rather than product development, the timeline collapses.

2. **Zero actual market validation.** Not a single person has paid for anything. Not a waitlist signup number. Not a social media follower count. Not a beta tester. The traction section is entirely about the founders' research output, not market demand signals. This is the most common failure mode I see in "research-first" founding teams -- they know everything about the market and nothing about whether anyone will pay them.

3. **Physical space dependency.** The break-even model requires EUR 10K/month from a physical space that does not exist, in a location that has not been scouted, with unit economics that have not been tested. If the space is delayed by 6 months (which is common in Berlin commercial real estate), the runway calculation changes materially.

None of these are absolute deal-breakers at pre-seed. But all three would need to be addressed before I wire money.

## 7. Comparison to Best Pre-Seed Pitch Books

**Research quality: Top 5% of what I have seen.**

The sourcing discipline, competitive depth, and analytical rigor are genuinely exceptional. Most pre-seed founders hand me 12 slides with unsourced market sizes and a "competitive advantage" slide that lists features. This is 26 pages of sourced, structured, analytical thinking. The design quality alone signals taste and attention to detail -- both of which matter in a lifestyle brand.

**Market validation: Bottom 25%.**

For a company at this stage with this level of polish, the absence of any market signal is conspicuous. The best pre-seed books I have funded included: a Typeform survey with 200+ responses showing willingness to pay, a WhatsApp group with 80 target users actively discussing the problem, a Shopify store with 30 pre-orders, or a landing page with 500 email signups. Alche has none of these. The research is exceptional, but research is not a proxy for demand.

**Overall: Top 20%.** The quality of thinking puts this ahead of 80% of what crosses my desk. The gap between thinking and doing keeps it out of the top 10%.

## 8. The Graveyard Integration

**Does the "$1.4B in failures" framing work?**

Mostly yes. The graveyard analysis is the book's secret weapon. It is the single element that most clearly separates this from a typical pre-seed pitch. Every health-tech investor has seen Forward die, has seen Arivale shut down, has watched DayTwo and Virta struggle. Showing that you have studied these failures and extracted operational principles (asset-light, content-led, community-first) is more convincing than any hockey-stick projection.

**Where it feels forced:** The Graveyard Rule sidebars in Section 07 (pages 14, 15) feel like they are being shoehorned into sections where they do not naturally belong. "Graveyard Rule #1" and "Graveyard Rule #4" read like a branding exercise applied to what should be straightforward business model commentary. The graveyard framing works brilliantly in the Competitive Landscape section where it has narrative room. In the Business Model section, it feels like a formatting decision rather than an insight.

**Suggested fix:** Keep the graveyard analysis concentrated in Sections 01 and 04 where it has the most narrative power. In other sections, reference the lessons without the "Graveyard Rule #N" branding. The insight is valuable; the labeling system makes it feel like a gimmick by the third occurrence.

---

## 9. The Ask Page

**Is the 1-page teaser effective?** Yes. This is well-executed.

EUR 500K / EUR 2.5M cap / 20% discount / Wandeldarlehen -- all standard, all clean. No exotic terms, no red flags. The three-card visual (EUR 500K / EUR 2.5M / 20%) is immediately scannable. The deal detail table below it is comprehensive without being overwhelming.

**Does it create urgency?** Moderately. The "12-18 month competitive window" is stated throughout the book, and the Ask page references it. But the urgency is undermined by the fact that the company has no product, no traction, and no committed CTO. The urgency argument works when there is something to compete over -- when you have early traction that could accelerate with capital. Here, the urgency is market-timing based, which is less compelling because the founders are not yet in a position to capitalize on the window.

**Would I want to see the detailed term sheet?** Yes. The "[Detailed allocation shared separately upon request]" is smart. It creates a reason for the next conversation. I would ask for the use-of-funds breakdown, the cap table, and the CTO candidate profile.

## 10. The Team Section

**Is the CTO gap handled well?** Better than most, but not well enough.

The framing is honest: "We do not have a technical co-founder yet. This is flagged, not hidden." That is the right tone. The mitigation is reasonable: both founders have technical degrees (BSc Computer Engineering, MSc Data Science), a candidate has been scoped, and a 16-week MVP plan exists.

**Does the "honest framing" feel genuine or performative?**

It lands somewhere in between. The honesty is genuine -- most founders at this stage would either hide the CTO gap or overstate a "technical advisor" as if they were a co-founder. The fact that they call it "The CTO Gap -- Honest Framing" and dedicate a callout box to it shows self-awareness.

But the framing also has a performative quality. By the time I reach Section 11, I have been told multiple times throughout the book that "funding unlocks this hire." The repetition starts to feel like the founders are trying to pre-empt my objection rather than address it. A more confident approach would be: state the gap once, state the plan once, and move on.

**Other team observations:**

- Timur's experience (EUR 1.8M Meta ad spend, 29M influencer views, 10M+ user app) is solid for a growth/marketing co-founder. The Concentrix/Meta role demonstrates he can manage paid acquisition at scale.
- Daria's bio is less concrete. "Built production backend systems" at which scale? "Established figure in longevity and wellness topics" -- established how? Published where? Followed by whom? This needs specifics. If Daria has a social following or published content in the longevity space, that IS traction and should be quantified.
- The medical advisor (Shukri Jarmoukli) is well-positioned -- a self-pay longevity practice in Berlin whose patients are the exact target demographic. This is a genuine strategic asset.
- The equity split (Daria 51% / Timur 49%) is close to equal, which usually signals either genuine partnership or unresolved power dynamics. At the meeting I would ask how decisions are made when they disagree.

## Final Assessment

This pitch book demonstrates exceptional analytical capability and genuine market understanding. The founders have done the work that most pre-seed teams skip entirely. The competitive analysis, graveyard framing, and problem articulation are best-in-class for this stage.

The fatal gap is between thinking and doing. The book reads like two founders who are brilliant strategists and rigorous researchers -- but I cannot tell if they can sell. Can they get 50 people into a room? Can they get 100 people to sign up for a waitlist? Can they get one person to pay EUR 19?

My advice to the founders: before you send this book to a single investor, publish your first piece of content, run your first event, and open your waitlist. Even if the numbers are tiny, real-world signals transform this pitch from "impressive research project" to "fundable company." Right now, this book asks me to bet EUR 500K on a hypothesis. Give me one data point -- just one -- that the hypothesis has legs.

I would take the first meeting. I would hold my checkbook until I see a signal from the market, not just from the founders.

**Rating: 7.5 / 10**

(For context: the best pre-seed pitch book I have funded was an 8.5. It had worse design, worse research, but it came with 200 waitlist signups and a working prototype that three people were paying for.)
