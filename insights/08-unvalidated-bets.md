# Insight: Five Unvalidated Bets That Need Customer Interview Data

## These are the assumptions that could break the thesis. None have been tested with real customers yet.

---

### Bet 1: "Imperfect Longevity" Resonates as a Purchasing Motivation

**The assumption:** People are tired of optimization culture and will pay for a longevity brand that feels human, joyful, and non-judgmental.

**The risk:** People who currently spend money on longevity are optimization-oriented by definition. "Imperfect" may attract browsers who never buy, not customers who pay $50-100/mo. The gap between stated preference ("I love this brand!") and actual spending behavior ("but I won't pay for it") is the graveyard of consumer brands.

**How to test:** Concept boards with 3 positioning variants. Pricing willingness questions paired with concept reactions.

---

### Bet 2: Food Is the Social Object for Longevity Community

**The assumption:** Biomarker data is too private to share socially. Food (cooking, eating, sharing meals) is the universal social object that unlocks community around longevity.

**The risk:** Nobody has built longevity community at scale. The structural challenges are real:
- Data sensitivity: HIGH (biomarkers vs. step counts)
- Sharing comfort: LOW ("my testosterone is low" vs. "I ran a 5K!")
- Engagement frequency: QUARTERLY (tests) vs. DAILY (fitness)
- Identity formation: UNDEFINED ("I am a longevity person"?)
- Proven model at scale: NONE

**How to test:** Pop-up food events in Berlin. Measure: attendance, social sharing, willingness to join waitlist, conversion from event to app signup.

---

### Bet 3: $50-100/mo Willingness to Pay

**The assumption:** The target segment will pay $50-100/mo for an integrated longevity membership.

**The risk:** This is the most contested price band in consumer longevity (Levels ~$65/mo, Rootine ~$70/mo, ZOE ~$60/mo). Below $50/mo requires 100K+ subscribers. Above $129/mo requires clinical depth. The middle is a compression zone.

**How to test:** Van Westendorp pricing sensitivity. Conjoint analysis on bundle features. Substitution mapping ("what would you stop spending on to afford this?").

---

### Bet 4: Women 30-55 Are the Entry Segment

**The assumption:** Women aged 30-55 in Berlin, wellness-engaged but alienated by biohacker culture, are the primary customer.

**The risk:**
- Gen Z drives 41% of U.S. wellness spend (McKinsey) -- are we missing a younger cohort?
- Is the gender segmentation correct? Some men also feel alienated by Bryan Johnson-style optimization.
- Berlin audience: expat, German, or mixed? Language and cultural framing depend on this.

**How to test:** Broad screening survey in Berlin. Demographic segmentation. Persona validation with 3-4 hypothetical personas.

---

### Bet 5: Community > Data as Retention Mechanism

**The assumption:** Community, identity, and in-person connection retain users longer than data dashboards and push notifications.

**The risk:** 90% wearable abandonment proves data-first fails. But community-first longevity has never been tested either. CrossFit and Peloton prove community retention for fitness -- but fitness has daily engagement, shareable achievements, and strong identity formation. Longevity may not.

**How to test:** Cohort retention tracking post-launch. A/B comparison of engagement between community-active and community-passive users.

---

## The Meta-Insight

These five bets are not independently risky -- they are *interdependent*. If Bet 1 fails (positioning doesn't resonate), Bets 2-5 don't matter. If Bet 2 fails (food isn't the social object), Bet 5 (community retention) collapses. The customer interview sequence should test them in order of dependency.
