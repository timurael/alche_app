## Key Industry Reports

### Global Wellness Summit 2026 Trends Report (January 2026)

**Status: VERIFIED.** 150-page report, longest-running wellness forecast.

Key findings relevant to Alche:

1. **"Powerful tension"** between high-tech, hyper-optimizing approaches and no-tech, deeply human wellness. The report frames this as *the* defining industry tension for 2026.

2. **Over-optimization backlash** named as a top trend: *"Wellness experiences will embrace what humans actually are: imperfect, emotional, relational and sensory."*

3. **Women's Longevity** identified as a distinct trend. Quote: the longevity market has been **"tacitly male."** The report states *"the future is female."*

4. **Neurowellness** as rising category.

5. **2026 framed as "a year of corrections and backlashes."**

Sources: [GWS, "10 Wellness Trends for 2026"](https://www.globalwellnesssummit.com/2026trends/); [PR Newswire announcement](https://www.prnewswire.com/news-releases/global-wellness-summit-releases-10-wellness-trends-for-2026-302670771.html)

### McKinsey "Future of Wellness" (2025)

**Status: VERIFIED.** Survey of 9,000+ consumers across US, UK, Germany, China.

Key findings:

1. **Six subcategories poised for growth:** functional nutrition, healthy aging, appearance/aesthetics, in-person wellness services, weight management, mental health.

2. **Consumer unmet needs** identified in cognitive health, mindfulness/mental health, and longevity -- especially Gen Z.

3. **Gen Z drives 41% of U.S. wellness spend** despite being ~36% of the adult population.

4. **84% of consumers** say wellness is a top day-to-day priority.

5. **57% of consumers** prioritize aging well more than 5 years ago.

Sources: [McKinsey, "Future of Wellness"](https://www.mckinsey.com/industries/consumer-packaged-goods/our-insights/future-of-wellness-trends); [McKinsey, "Five Dimensions of Tomorrow's Wellness Economy"](https://www.mckinsey.com/featured-insights/mckinsey-explainers/the-future-takes-shape-five-dimensions-of-tomorrows-wellness-economy)

## Investor Objection Map

Based on the Forbes failure analysis and longevity VC landscape, these are the objections Alche should prepare for:

### Objection 1: "The graveyard problem"

> *"Companies that tried clinical + lifestyle have a 0% success rate. Why are you different?"*

| Dead company | What they tried | Why they died | How Alche avoids it |
|---|---|---|---|
| Forward Health ($657M) | Own everything: hardware, clinics, AI | $1M/unit custom pods. $25K CAC. | Asset-light: partner for lab, Heilpraktiker, no custom hardware |
| Modern Age ($33M) | Premium clinical + aesthetic space | Unsustainable rent + clinical staffing | Cafe model: revenue-generating space, not cost center |
| Habit ($60M) | Test, cook, deliver food | Food supply chain is a nightmare | Curate, don't manufacture. Cafe + partner products |
| DayTwo ($85M) | World-class microbiome science | GLP-1 drugs evaporated use case | Community moat, not pure science moat |

Source: [Forbes/GWS analysis](https://www.globalwellnesssummit.com/blog/why-vc-backed-longevity-startups-are-dying-in-a-5-trillion-wellness-market/)

### Objection 2: "The TAM is smaller than you say"

Prepare two versions:
- **Conservative:** $23-32B global longevity market, 8-10% CAGR
- **Expansive (with justification):** European share of above, women's health subsector ($40B menopause market by 2030), community/subscription model addressable

### Objection 3: "What about ZOE / Hims / Neko?"

See detailed counterexample analysis in [`02-competitive-landscape.md`](02-competitive-landscape.md). Have slide-ready answers for each.

### Objection 4: "Community is unproven in longevity"

**Honest response:** "Yes, and that's what this pre-seed round tests. Our hypothesis is that food -- not biomarkers -- is the social object that makes longevity community viable. Here's our 6-month test plan."

### Objection 5: "GLP-1 drugs could make this irrelevant"

**Honest response:** "DayTwo proved that science alone doesn't survive disruption. Community, identity, and daily engagement are the assets that survive. We're building those."

### Objection 6: "Unit economics at $50-100/mo"

Prepare detailed unit economics showing:
- Cafe revenue as marketing spend offset
- App subscription as primary margin driver
- Lab partnerships as variable cost (not fixed)
- Community events as retention driver (LTV impact)

### Objection 7: "Why Berlin?"

- ANTI Berlin proves demand for science-meets-lifestyle in the city
- Berlin Biohackers meetup: 2,213 members
- Two major longevity summits in 2026 (April + May)
- Lower operational costs than London, Paris, or US cities
- Emerging European longevity hub

### Objection 8: "Regulatory risk"

The Heilpraktiker bridge (German alternative health practitioner licensing) allows certain services without full medical licensure. **However:** this is Germany-specific and doesn't scale to other markets without adaptation. Prepare a regulatory pathway slide for expansion.

### What surviving companies have in common

Per the Forbes analysis:

> *"The startups that survive will have to blend medical rigor, operational resilience, and consumer empathy, and prove not just that their protocols feel good -- but that they work."*

Key survival traits:
- Asset-light operations (partner, don't build)
- Recurring revenue model (subscription, not one-off)
- Clear clinical evidence (not just marketing claims)
- Consumer empathy in brand positioning
- Operational resilience (low fixed costs)
