## The Five Critical Questions

### Q1: Does "Imperfect Longevity" resonate -- or repel?

**What we assume:** People are tired of optimization culture and want a longevity brand that feels human, joyful, and non-judgmental.

**What we don't know:**
- Do real consumers in our target segment (women 30-55, Berlin, wellness-engaged) actually use the word "imperfect" positively -- or does it signal low quality?
- Is the backlash against optimization real at the purchase level, or just a cultural conversation that doesn't translate to spending?
- Does "joyful longevity" motivate action, or is it too soft? Do people want *permission to be imperfect*, or do they want *help becoming better*?

**How to test:**
- Concept boards: 3 positioning variants (Imperfect Longevity vs. Joyful Science vs. Warm Rigor)
- Unstructured interviews: "Tell me about the last wellness product you bought. What made you choose it?"
- Reaction test: Show competitor brands (ZOE, Function Health, Goop). Ask where respondents place themselves.

**Decision unlocked:** Positioning language and brand tone.

---

### Q2: Is food the social object that makes longevity community viable?

**What we assume:** Biomarker data is too private to share socially. Food is the universal social object -- cooking, eating, sharing meals -- that unlocks community around longevity without the vulnerability of medical data.

**What we don't know:**
- Will people actually share longevity-food experiences publicly (post about it, invite friends, attend events)?
- Is a "longevity cafe" different enough from a health food cafe (Daluma, etc.) to justify the positioning?
- What's the conversion path from "cafe visitor" to "app subscriber"?

**How to test:**
- Pop-up event: Host 2-3 longevity food events in Berlin. Measure: attendance, sharing behavior (social posts, word-of-mouth), willingness to join a waitlist.
- Interview probe: "If you learned that a specific meal could improve a biomarker, would you share that with friends? Post about it? Want to cook it together?"
- Prototype the cafe-to-app funnel: physical card → QR → app signup. Measure conversion rate.

**Decision unlocked:** Whether the cafe is the right anchor, or whether another entry point (app, products, events) should lead.

---

### Q3: Is there a willingness to pay $50-100/mo for this?

**What we assume:** The target segment will pay $50-100/mo for an integrated longevity membership that includes app access, community, and periodic biomarker insights.

**What we don't know:**
- Is this price anchored correctly? Do consumers compare it to: a gym ($50-80), a Netflix subscription ($15), a health app ($10-30), or a clinical service ($200+)?
- What would make someone choose Alche over free alternatives (Huberman podcast + MyFitnessPal + an annual blood test)?
- Is there a "first year" vs. "ongoing" willingness-to-pay gap?

**How to test:**
- Van Westendorp pricing sensitivity: "At what price would this be too expensive? A bargain? Starting to get expensive? Too cheap to trust?"
- Conjoint analysis: Which bundle features drive willingness to pay? (Community, biomarkers, food, app, events)
- Competitive reference: "You currently spend $X/mo on wellness. Would you replace any of it with this?"

**Decision unlocked:** Pricing tier structure, what must be included in base vs. premium.

---

### Q4: Who is the actual entry customer?

**What we assume:** Women aged 30-55 in Berlin, wellness-engaged, longevity-curious but alienated by biohacker culture.

**What we don't know:**
- Is the primary motivation *longevity* (living longer/healthier) or *wellness* (feeling good now)? These drive very different purchasing behaviors.
- Is the gender segmentation correct? Are there men who also feel alienated by Bryan Johnson-style optimization but want science-backed longevity?
- Is the age range right? Gen Z drives 41% of U.S. wellness spend (McKinsey) -- are we missing a younger cohort?
- Berlin-specific: Is the audience expat, German, or mixed? Language and cultural framing depend on this.

**How to test:**
- Screening survey: Broad demographic reach in Berlin. Screen for: wellness spending, longevity awareness, brand associations, technology comfort.
- Persona validation: Present 3-4 hypothetical personas. Ask: "Which of these is most like you?"
- Geographic probe: Where in Berlin? Mitte/Prenzlauer Berg (hipster wellness), Charlottenburg (premium), Kreuzberg (counterculture)?

**Decision unlocked:** Target segment definition, geographic focus for first location, marketing channel strategy.

---

### Q5: What is the actual competitive set in consumers' minds?

**What we assume:** Consumers see the market the way our 4-axis map describes it -- split between science/cold and warm/shallow.

**What we don't know:**
- Do consumers even think in terms of "longevity" -- or is that a VC/industry word? They might think "health," "aging well," "wellness," or "self-care."
- What do consumers see as substitutes? Is Alche competing with ZOE, or with a gym membership, or with a nutritionist, or with a cookbook?
- Is the competitive frame *category* (longevity brands) or *occasion* (Saturday morning health ritual, quarterly check-in)?

**How to test:**
- Free association: "When I say 'longevity,' what comes to mind? When I say 'aging well'? When I say 'health optimization'?"
- Substitution mapping: "If Alche didn't exist, what would you use instead? What would you stop spending on to afford this?"
- Unprompted positioning: Show the Alche concept (without the map). Ask: "What other brands does this remind you of? Who would you compare this to?"

**Decision unlocked:** Messaging vocabulary, competitive differentiation angle, category definition.

## Interview Design Recommendations

### Sample

| Segment | N | Recruiting criteria |
|---|---|---|
| Primary: Women 30-45, Berlin, wellness-active | 8-10 | Spend >EUR 50/mo on wellness, aware of longevity as concept |
| Secondary: Women 45-55, Berlin, health-focused | 5-7 | Annual health check-ups, interested in aging well |
| Exploratory: Men 30-45, Berlin, wellness-curious | 3-5 | To test gender assumption |
| Exploratory: Gen Z 22-29, Berlin, wellness-native | 3-5 | To test age assumption |

### Format

- 45-60 minute semi-structured interviews
- Mix of unstructured ("tell me about...") and structured (concept boards, pricing, brand comparisons)
- In-person preferred (Berlin cafes). Video acceptable.
- Record and transcribe (with consent)

### Sequence

1. **Warm-up:** Current wellness behavior, spending, routines (5 min)
2. **Language probe:** Free association with longevity/aging/wellness terms (5 min)
3. **Competitor reactions:** Show 5-6 brand screenshots (ZOE, Function Health, Goop, AG1, WHOOP, Noom). Reactions and self-placement. (10 min)
4. **Concept test:** Show 2-3 Alche positioning variants. Unstructured reactions. (10 min)
5. **Community probe:** "Would you share this? Attend this? Bring a friend?" (5 min)
6. **Pricing:** Van Westendorp or direct WTP questions. (5 min)
7. **Wrap-up:** "What would make you say yes? What would make you say no?" (5 min)
