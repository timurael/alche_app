## 1. SECTION PRIORITY RANKING BY INVESTOR ATTENTION WEIGHT

European pre-seed angels allocate approximately 12 minutes to a pitch book before deciding whether to take a meeting. The order below reflects where those minutes go, not where founders think they go.

| Priority | Section | Attention Weight | Rationale |
|----------|---------|-----------------|-----------|
| 1 | **The Team** | 30% | At pre-seed, this is the investment. No product, no traction, no revenue. Angels bet on people. This section must answer: "Can these two execute?" |
| 2 | **The Ask** (Teaser + Separate PDFs) | 20% | European angels immediately check the structure, valuation, and terms. They want to know if the deal is reasonable before reading anything else. |
| 3 | **Business Model & Unit Economics** | 15% | European investors are structurally conservative. They want to see capital efficiency, break-even path, and realistic margins before dream-state market sizing. |
| 4 | **The Insight / Problem** | 10% | Must land in under 30 seconds. Is there a real problem? Does the founder understand it in a non-obvious way? |
| 5 | **Solution / Product** | 8% | Briefly: what is the product, and how does it connect to the insight? NOT a feature tour. |
| 6 | **GTM Strategy** | 7% | Content-first is the strongest pre-seed GTM because it requires zero capital and produces measurable proof. Show the flywheel, not the tactics. |
| 7 | **Market** | 4% | At pre-seed, TAM/SAM/SOM slides are credibility signals, not decision drivers. Keep it tight. |
| 8 | **Graveyard / Lessons** | 3% | Reframed as "What $1.4B Taught Us" -- this is a differentiator, not a section investors expect. Its power is in demonstrating founder diligence. |
| 9 | **Competitive Landscape** | 2% | At pre-seed, this is about showing awareness, not exhaustive analysis. |
| 10 | **The Moat** | 1% | At pre-seed, no moat exists. The honest framing in the current draft ("we don't claim a moat, we have a thesis") is correct. Keep it to a half-page. |
| 11 | **Roadmap** | <1% | The roadmap only matters if the investor already believes in the team, the insight, and the economics. It is a confirmation slide, not a persuasion slide. |

## 5. THE ASK TEASER VALIDATION

### The 1-page teaser approach is CORRECT.

The claude.md instruction states: "Mark as '[Detailed allocation shared separately upon request]' -- do NOT include full use-of-funds breakdown in the book." This is the right call for three reasons:

1. **Jurisdiction complexity**: German Wandeldarlehen terms differ from US convertible note terms. A single Ask page with full terms will confuse investors from the wrong jurisdiction and create legal risk.
2. **European angel psychology**: DACH angels prefer to negotiate terms in person. Showing a fixed term sheet in a pitch book signals inflexibility. The teaser creates a conversation opener.
3. **Information asymmetry**: Detailed fund allocation invites line-item objections. "Why is marketing 18%?" is a debate you want to have in a meeting, not a reason for an investor to decline a meeting.

### Teaser Structure (1 Page)

The teaser page in the pitch book should contain ONLY:

- **Instrument**: Wandeldarlehen (Convertible Loan)
- **Raise target**: EUR 500K
- **Valuation cap**: EUR 2.5M
- **Discount**: 20%
- **Use in one sentence**: "18-month runway to 500 paying subscribers and seed-readiness."
- **Call to action**: "Detailed term sheet, use of funds, and jurisdiction-specific documentation shared upon request."

### Separate PDFs (Not in the Pitch Book)

Two PDFs, prepared but not included in the pitch book:

1. **German/DACH PDF**: Full Wandeldarlehen terms, GmbH structure, 3-tranche milestone detail (EUR 200K signing / EUR 150K at MVP+100 users / EUR 150K at EUR 10K MRR), German-language legal framing.
2. **US/International PDF**: Convertible note equivalent terms, USD-denominated cap, SAFE-comparable framing, cross-border investment mechanics.

These go to investors who request them after the pitch book creates interest. They should never be bundled with the pitch book itself.

## 6. RED FLAGS TO AVOID -- PHRASES AND FRAMINGS THAT REPEL EUROPEAN ANGELS

### Language Red Flags

| Avoid | Why | Replace With |
|-------|-----|-------------|
| "Disrupting healthcare" | European angels hear regulatory risk. | "Filling the prevention gap consumers are already paying to fill." |
| "We're the Uber/Airbnb of longevity" | Exhausted analogy. Signals shallow thinking. | Drop the analogy entirely. Describe what you are. |
| "AI-powered" as a leading claim | At pre-seed with no AI built, this is a liability, not an asset. | "Data-informed protocols" or "personalized based on biomarker data." Use AI in the roadmap (Phase 3+), not in the value proposition. |

| "Content CAC: EUR 0" | Technically true but reads as naive. Content costs founder time, which has opportunity cost. | "Content acquisition runs on founder labor, not capital. Our pre-funding CAC is effectively zero in direct spend." |
| "Four People, No Gaps" | Demonstrably false. There is no CTO and the first hire is TBD. An investor who reads this and then sees the TBD placeholder will question honesty. | "Four people covering product, growth, engineering, and medical. CTO is the first funded hire." |
| "We reverse-engineered our model from $1.4B worth of failure data" | Sounds academic. Founders study failures; investors fund builders. | "We studied what killed seven companies with better funding than us. Then we designed our model to avoid their structural mistakes." |
| Revenue projections beyond 18 months | Year 4+ projections (EUR 60M ARR, EUR 250M+ valuation) are not credible at pre-seed and make the founders look inexperienced. | Remove entirely. European angels penalize over-projection more than US angels do. |
| "10x return potential" | European angels find explicit return promises gauche. | Show the math implicitly: valuation cap, break-even timeline, seed raise target. Let the investor calculate the return. |

### Structural Red Flags

1. **Inconsistent data across slides**: If the Insight slide says 3.1% prevention spending and the locked data says 4.8%, investors will catch it. Harmonize all figures BEFORE distribution.
2. **Physical space contradiction**: If one slide says "digital-first, no buildout" and another allocates EUR 150K to a physical space, the pitch book contradicts itself. This is the #1 structural red flag.
3. **Placeholder text visible in the PDF**: The team section has yellow-bordered TBD boxes and "COMPENSATION STRUCTURE NEEDED" in monospace font. This must be resolved before any investor sees the document.
4. **Over-length**: A pitch book should be 15-20 pages for pre-seed. The current content, if printed as-is, would exceed 40 pages. European angels do not read 40-page pitch books. They decline the meeting.

## 7. TITLE GAP ASSESSMENT -- NO CEO TITLE

### Current State

- Timur Tasdogan: "Co-Founder, 49%"
- Daria Dijan Roth: "Co-Founder, 51%"
- No CEO title assigned anywhere in the team section
- The claude.md reference file lists Timur as "Co-founder & CEO" with a note "Marketer. Brand strategy, design, community."
- The governance table shows domain split: Timur (brand, data, growth) vs. Daria (vision, architecture, ops) with shared product responsibility

### Assessment: This IS a Red Flag -- But a Fixable One

**Why it matters for investors**:
- European angels (especially German ones) expect clear leadership hierarchy. The GmbH structure requires a Geschaeftsfuehrer (Managing Director). If neither founder is identified as CEO/GF, investors will ask: "Who makes the final call when you disagree?"
- At 49/51 equity split with no CEO title, the power dynamic is ambiguous. Is Daria the majority owner and therefore the de facto leader? Or is the 49/51 split cosmetic (visa/legal reasons)?
- The "10-year personal friendship" framing is a double-edged sword. Investors have seen friendships end over startup disagreements. Without clear authority structures, the friendship becomes a risk factor, not an asset.

**Why the current approach might be intentional**:
- Some pre-seed founders avoid CEO titles to signal collaborative, flat culture.
- In DACH startup culture, "Co-Founder" without a hierarchical title is more common than in the US.
- The domain split (brand/growth vs. vision/architecture) is clear enough that a CEO title may feel redundant.

### Recommendation: Assign the CEO Title to Timur

The claude.md already identifies Timur as CEO. The pitch book should reflect this. Here is why:

1. **GmbH requirement**: The company needs a Geschaeftsfuehrer. Identifying who that is now signals operational maturity.
2. **Investor expectation**: When angels ask "who is the CEO?" -- and they will -- the founders should not have to explain. The pitch book should answer the question before it is asked.
3. **The 49/51 split already handles the power balance**: Daria has majority equity. Timur can hold the CEO title without disrupting the ownership dynamic. This is a common structure.
4. **Presentation framing**: "Timur Tasdogan, Co-Founder & CEO" and "Daria Dijan Roth, Co-Founder & COO" is clean, standard, and answers every governance question an investor would have.

If the founders prefer not to use CEO/COO titles, they must at minimum:
- Add a "Decision Framework" row to the governance table explaining how deadlocks are resolved
- Explicitly state who the Geschaeftsfuehrer will be
- Frame the 49/51 split as intentional with a one-sentence rationale

Leaving the current ambiguity in place will cost meetings.

3. **The unresolved placeholders.** TBD boxes, yellow-bordered placeholder text, and missing compensation structures signal that the team is not ready to raise. Every placeholder must be resolved or removed before distribution.

Fix these three, and the pitch book is fundable. Leave them, and investors will decline the meeting without explaining why.
