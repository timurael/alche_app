The thesis

Function Health reached $2.5B doing one thing for one market. Neko reached $1.8B doing one thing in two cities. The integrated European longevity platform doesn't exist yet.

User Intelligence
The same six complaints
across every competitor.
We analyzed user reviews across Trustpilot, Reddit, app stores, and Glassdoor. The pattern is structural, not accidental.
01
"Data without implementation"InsideTracker (4.1/5, 616 reviews): users say recommendations are generic lists with no coaching to act on them.
Testing companies identify problems but send users away without a plan.
02
"Expensive for what you get"InsideTracker $340-589/test, Function $365/yr, YEARS Berlin $3,000-16,000+.
Science-first brands price out the mainstream.
03
"I stopped using it after a few months"3.3% health app retention at day 30. 29-30% wearable abandonment.
Data dashboards don't sustain behavior change.
04
"It's not for people like me"Blueprint's $1M "Immortals" tier. Equinox Optimize at $40K/yr. Biohacker aesthetics coded male/extreme.
The market has been "tacitly male" (GWS 2026).
05
"I can't tell what's real science"AG1: 100+ FDA adverse event reports, disputed by Harvard and McGill. Goop: FDA warning letters.
Warm brands have a credibility problem. Clinical brands have a warmth problem.
06
"My phone already tracks this"Apple Health, Google Health Connect, Samsung Health -- all free, all improving.
Every standalone health app faces the platform aggregation threat.

What these complaints reveal

The market doesn't need another testing service, wearable, or supplement. It needs someone to connect the dots -- testing + action + community + sustained engagement -- in a way that feels human and accessible. That's the integration thesis.

Learning from the Dead
Execution failures, not market failures.
Every failed company made at least one of three mistakes: owning the entire value chain, confusing science with product, or building without community.

Mistake #1
Owned the entire value chain. Forward ($1M pods), Habit (test-cook-deliver). Full vertical integration in healthcare is ruinously expensive.
Mistake #2
Confused science with product. DayTwo had 2,089 citations. Still died. InsideTracker has MIT founders. Still ~$3.8M ARR after 15 years.
Mistake #3
No community moat. When GLP-1 arrived, DayTwo had nothing. When users got bored of Oura, no social reason to stay.

Our structural response

Partner, don't build. Lab partnerships for testing. Product curation, not manufacturing. The cafe generates revenue; it's not a cost center. Community is the product, not an add-on. If our community hypothesis validates, social fabric becomes the moat that survives GLP-1 disruptions, wearable commoditization, and big tech encroachment.

Forward View
The 2029 landscape, not today's.
By 2029, most longevity data will be commoditized. The companies that built genuine community and cultural identity will have the strongest retention and lowest CAC.
2026-2027
Neko, Lanserhof, CLP fund see deal flow. Consolidation begins. Well-funded players acquire digital platforms with weak distribution.
2027-2028
EU member states pilot insurance reimbursement for preventive longevity diagnostics. Partial coverage dramatically expands addressable market.
2028-2029
Blood tests cheaper. AI interpretation better. Wearables more accurate. Data is commoditized. Community is the only moat that survives.

The Takeaway
What this map tells an investor.

Nobody is doing rigorous, multi-dimensional longevity science with warm, inclusive positioning, a physical space anchor, and community architecture at an accessible price point -- especially in Europe, especially centering women.

What we've learned from the dead
Partner, don't build the whole stack.
Community is the product, not an add-on.
The cafe generates revenue, it's not a cost center.
Data gets commoditized. Identity doesn't.
Science is necessary but not sufficient.
Speed beats capital in early markets.
The question isn't "will integration work?" It's "will we execute?"
The companies that tried the whole thing and failed all made specific, identifiable, avoidable mistakes.
That's what pre-seed capital is for.
