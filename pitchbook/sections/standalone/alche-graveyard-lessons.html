Part 2 Goes on the slide
The 7 Graveyard Rules
Every one of these deaths maps to a structural pattern. Not bad luck, not bad timing, not bad management -- structural architectural choices that made failure nearly inevitable regardless of execution quality. We distilled these into 7 rules that governed every design decision in Alche's model.
01
Closed Loop Trap
Never own both diagnostics AND food production. They have incompatible cost structures, incompatible margins, and incompatible operational complexity. The moment you're running a lab and a kitchen, you're running two companies.
Violators: Habit ($60M+), DayTwo ($85M)
02
Front-Loading Curse
If the most valuable moment in your product is the initial test or onboarding, your subscriptions will bleed. The value must compound over time, not peak on day one.
Violators: Habit ($60M+), Arivale ($50M), DayTwo ($85M)
03
Preventive Health Paradox
Consumers will not pay sustained premiums for future health benefits they cannot feel in the present. You need tangible, felt value -- energy, sleep, clarity, community -- not just risk reduction projections.
Violators: Arivale ($50M), Forward ($657M)
04
Two-Business Death Spiral
Running a tech company and a physical operations company simultaneously requires 2-3x the capital at half the speed. You end up underinvesting in both instead of excelling at either.
Violators: Forward ($657M), Habit ($60M+)
05
Identity Crisis Kill Zone
Pivoting between medical, wellness, and enterprise positioning exhausts capital and confuses the market. Pick a lane and stay in it. Every pivot costs 6-12 months and millions in repositioning.
Violators: DayTwo (4 pivots, $85M), Forward ($657M)
06
Reimbursement Wall
If your business model requires creating a novel insurance reimbursement category, budget $100M+ and 5-10 years. Pear proved clinicians prescribe, patients engage, outcomes improve -- and payers still wouldn't pay.
Violators: Pear ($400M+), DayTwo ($85M)
07
Hardware Multiplier
Physical hardware multiplies every risk: regulatory approval timelines, manufacturing defects, supply chain dependency, safety liability. Forward's CarePods had patients getting stuck inside. Proteus spent nearly two decades on an ingestible sensor.
Violators: Forward ($657M), Proteus ($532M)
Part 3 The Living
What survivors did differently
Five companies in the same space that are still alive, still growing, still funded. The pattern is ruthlessly simple: own one piece of the value chain and connect to existing infrastructure for everything else.
ZOE
~$83M raised
Science-first go-to-market. Published in Nature Medicine before launching a product. Then pivoted their COVID symptom tracker (4.5M+ contributors at zero customer acquisition cost) into a nutrition platform. Over 100K paying members. No meal delivery -- they tell you what to eat, they don't cook it for you.
Sources: TechCrunch (Dec 2022), ZOE press releases, Deloitte Fast 50 (2024), Nature Medicine publications
Levels
~$21M raised
Content-first community. Built a massive audience before building a product, then made the CGM hardware optional rather than core. Disciplined $0.5M/month burn rate gave them 2+ years of runway. The insight: the community is the product, the hardware is a feature.
Sources: Levels blog, a]16z Future podcast interviews, Crunchbase
Viome
~$75M raised
Learn from others' deaths. Acquired Habit's microbiome data after Habit died, immediately killed Habit's meal delivery business, and pivoted to supplements. Now in CVS retail distribution. Took the data asset, abandoned the operational nightmare.
Sources: Viome press releases, Crunchbase, industry reporting
Fay
$75M raised
Marketplace model, zero COGS. Dietitian marketplace using existing insurance reimbursement codes -- no novel category creation needed. 2,300+ dietitians. No proprietary content creation, no hardware, no physical spaces. 5x valuation increase in one year.
Sources: Fay press releases, Crunchbase, digital health reporting
AG1
$600M+ revenue
Single-SKU supplement, 10+ years of content. One product. $2.2M+ annual podcast advertising spend. Over a decade of audience-building before the business reached its current scale. Content-first, commerce-second. They own the audience, not the supply chain complexity.
Sources: AG1 public financials, podcast advertising reports, industry analysis

The survivor pattern

Every survivor owns one piece of the value chain and connects to existing infrastructure for the rest. ZOE owns science + recommendations, not food. Levels owns community + content, not hardware. Fay owns the marketplace, not the dietitians. AG1 owns the audience, not manufacturing. The dead tried to own everything.

Part 4 Goes on the slide
How Alche avoids every trap
We didn't design Alche's model from first principles and hope for the best. We reverse-engineered it from $1.4B worth of failure data. Every structural decision maps to a specific graveyard rule.
Rule 01: Closed Loop Trap
Habit and DayTwo died combining diagnostics with food production.
Alche's Design
We partner with existing lab providers for bloodwork and connect to existing wearable ecosystems. We recommend protocols -- we don't cook meals or ship test kits.
Rule 02: Front-Loading Curse
Arivale peaked at the initial test. After that, no reason to stay.
Alche's Design
Value compounds over time. Longitudinal data tracking, evolving personalized protocols, community belonging, and content library that grows. Month 6 is more valuable than Month 1.
Rule 03: Preventive Paradox
Consumers won't pay for future benefits they can't feel today.
Alche's Design
Immediate felt value: community events, daily content, energy/sleep/mood tracking with visible trends. Longevity is the mission, but the daily experience is what keeps people paying.
Rule 04: Two-Business Death Spiral
Forward burned $657M running tech + physical clinics simultaneously.
Alche's Design
Digital-first. The app is the business. The Berlin physical space is a single proof-of-concept location that drives acquisition and retention -- not a clinic chain we need to scale.
Rule 05: Identity Crisis Kill Zone
DayTwo pivoted 4 times across consumer, enterprise, clinical, and back.
Alche's Design
We're a consumer wellness platform. Not medical. Not enterprise. Not clinical. Our positioning -- anti-perfectionist longevity for real life -- is locked and validated across 3 research cycles.
Rule 06: Reimbursement Wall
Pear spent $400M+ and still couldn't get payers to cover digital therapeutics.
Alche's Design
Direct-to-consumer subscriptions. Zero insurance dependency. EUR 19-99/mo price points are consumer discretionary, not medical reimbursement. We never need a payer to say yes.
Rule 07: Hardware Multiplier
Proteus spent $532M and 20 years on an ingestible sensor. Forward's CarePods malfunctioned.
Alche's Design
No proprietary hardware. We integrate with existing wearables (Oura, Apple Watch, Garmin) and existing CGM providers. We're a software layer on top of hardware others build and maintain.

Slide direction
Visualization Direction -- Slide

Option A: Graveyard Visual. A minimal table or card layout showing the 7 companies on the left (faded, crossed out, in muted red) with capital figures. On the right, the 5 survivors in sage/green. Between them, the 7 rules as connecting threads. The visual story: death on the left, life on the right, rules in the middle. Alche's position clearly mapped to the right side.

Option B: The Architecture Split. A simple two-column visual. Left column: "What we own" (data, community, content, brand). Right column: "What we partner for" (labs, hardware, services). Below, a crossed-out row: "What we never touch" (food production, hardware manufacturing, insurance billing). Clean, editorial, instantly readable. This is the strongest single-slide distillation.

Option C: Rule Checklist. The 7 rules as a vertical list with red X marks next to dead companies and green checkmarks next to Alche's design choices. This plays well in presentation because you can reveal each rule one at a time, building tension before showing how Alche addresses each one. Works best as an animated slide.

Delivery Notes

This is a confidence slide, not a data slide. The goal isn't to impress investors with how many failures you've studied. The goal is to communicate: "we are not naive about how hard this is, and we designed our model to avoid the structural mistakes that killed companies with 100x our funding." That's the emotional residue. If an investor walks away thinking "these founders have done their homework," this slide did its job.

In the presentation, consider opening with: "$1.4 billion in health-tech capital was destroyed by 7 companies. We studied every one of them. Here's what we learned." Then walk through 2-3 rules maximum (not all 7 -- save the rest for Q&A). The investor will ask about the others. That's the point.
