## Experiment 1: Concept Testing (Detailed Design)

### Objective
Determine which positioning variant drives highest purchase intent at EUR 49/mo.

### Materials

**Variant A: "Imperfect Longevity"**
- Headline: "Longevity is better imperfect than never."
- Subhead: "Science-backed longevity for women who want to age well -- without the pressure to be perfect."
- Image: Women laughing at a table with functional food. Warm lighting. No lab coats.
- Features listed: Community events, AI-powered guidance, biomarker testing, cafe space
- Price shown: EUR 49/mo

**Variant B: "Science-Informed Longevity"**
- Headline: "Know your body. Trust the science."
- Subhead: "Evidence-based longevity guidance, personalized to your biology."
- Image: Clean, bright science-meets-lifestyle aesthetic. Woman looking at biomarker results with a smile.
- Features listed: Same as A
- Price shown: EUR 49/mo

**Variant C: "Community Longevity"**
- Headline: "Aging well is better together."
- Subhead: "Join the community of women rewriting what longevity looks like."
- Image: Group of women at an event, engaged in conversation.
- Features listed: Same as A
- Price shown: EUR 49/mo

### Protocol
1. Show each respondent all 3 variants (randomized order)
2. After each variant:
   - Appeal rating (1-10)
   - "How likely would you be to subscribe?" (Definitely / Probably / Maybe / Probably not / Definitely not)
   - "What's the ONE thing that would make you more likely to sign up?"
3. After all 3:
   - "Which would you most likely subscribe to?" (forced choice)
   - "Why?"
4. Van Westendorp questions (see Experiment 2)

### Sample
- 50+ women, ages 30-55
- Berlin-based
- Currently spend on wellness (yoga, supplements, nutrition, skincare)
- Recruited via: Instagram, Berlin wellness community groups, Longevity Germany, Meetup, personal networks

### Analysis
- Win/loss by variant (forced choice)
- Purchase intent distribution by variant
- Appeal-to-intent gap (high appeal but low purchase intent = "nice but won't pay")
- Qualitative themes from "what would make you subscribe"

### Decision Rules
| Result | Action |
|---|---|
| One variant clearly wins (>50% forced choice + >40% purchase intent) | Adopt that positioning |
| Two variants tied | Combine winning elements |
| All variants below 25% purchase intent at EUR 49 | Positioning needs fundamental rework |
| High appeal, low intent across all | Problem is value delivery/trust, not positioning |

## Experiment 2: Pricing Study (Detailed Design)

### Objective
Find the optimal price point and acceptable price range for Alche's subscription.

### Van Westendorp Questions
1. "At what monthly price would Alche be SO EXPENSIVE that you would not consider subscribing?" (too expensive)
2. "At what monthly price would you consider Alche to be SO CHEAP that you'd question its quality?" (too cheap)
3. "At what monthly price would Alche start to get expensive, but you'd still consider it?" (expensive/high)
4. "At what monthly price would you say Alche is a BARGAIN -- great value for money?" (cheap/good value)

### Substitution Mapping
5. "If you subscribed to Alche at EUR 49/mo, which of these would you reduce or cancel?"
   - Yoga studio membership
   - Supplement subscriptions (AG1, etc.)
   - Nutritionist visits
   - Wellness app subscriptions
   - Skincare products
   - None -- I'd add it to my current spending
   - Other: ________

### Analysis
- Plot Van Westendorp price curves
- Identify: Point of Marginal Cheapness, Point of Marginal Expensiveness, Optimal Price Point, Indifference Price Point
- Substitution patterns (what gets replaced, what doesn't)

### Decision Rules
| Result | Action |
|---|---|
| Optimal price EUR 35-65 | Proceed with tiered pricing as designed |
| Optimal price EUR 20-35 | Adjust tiers downward (EUR 15/29/59) |
| Optimal price <EUR 20 | Business model needs fundamental rethink |
| Strong substitution patterns | Feature those replacements in marketing ("Replace your [X] with Alche") |
| No substitution (incremental spend) | Higher CAC; adjust GTM accordingly |

## Experiment 3: Pop-Up Events (Detailed Design)

### Objective
Test whether food-centered events create community bonds and drive digital conversion.

### Event Format
- **Name:** "The Long Table" (working title)
- **Size:** 15-25 women per event
- **Duration:** 2.5 hours
- **Structure:**
  - 30 min: Arrival, welcome drink (functional beverage), mingling
  - 45 min: 3-course functional food dinner with ingredient cards
  - 30 min: Guest expert mini-talk (15 min) + Q&A (15 min)
  - 30 min: Facilitated table conversation (structured prompts)
  - 15 min: Wrap-up, app/waitlist signup, next event announcement
- **Price:** EUR 35-45 per ticket
- **Location:** Partner space (ANTI Berlin, co-working space with kitchen, or pop-up venue)

### Data Collection (Per Event)
- Ticket sales timeline (how fast did it sell?)
- Attendance rate (showed up / bought)
- Pre-event survey (current wellness spending, longevity awareness)
- Post-event survey (NPS, "would attend again," "would subscribe," WTP)
- Social sharing (Instagram tags, stories, posts within 48 hours)
- App/waitlist signups (tracked via unique QR code)
- Follow-up: 1-week post-event survey (what did you remember? what did you act on?)

### Success Metrics
| Metric | Target | Red Flag |
|---|---|---|
| Tickets sold (per event) | 20+ (85%+ capacity) | <12 (50% capacity) |
| NPS | >50 | <30 |
| "Would attend again" | >85% | <60% |
| Social sharing (within 48hrs) | >30% of attendees | <10% |
| Waitlist/app signup | >50% of attendees | <20% |
| "Would subscribe at EUR 49/mo" | >30% of attendees | <15% |

## Experiment 4: Customer Discovery Interviews (Detailed Design)

### Objective
Validate customer segment, understand needs/pains, test personas.

### Interview Guide (45-60 min)

**Part 1: Current behavior (15 min)**
- Walk me through your typical wellness/health routine in a week.
- What do you spend money on for health/wellness each month? (List everything)
- What's the one thing you wish was better about your health approach?

**Part 2: Longevity awareness (10 min)**
- When you hear "longevity," what comes to mind?
- Have you used any longevity-related products or services? (Prompt: OURA, ZOE, Function, supplements)
- What do you think of the biohacking/optimization movement?

**Part 3: Community and social (10 min)**
- Do you do any health/wellness activities with others? What?
- Have you ever wanted a community around health/aging well?
- What would make you attend a health-related event regularly?

**Part 4: Concept reaction (15 min)**
- Show concept board (winning variant from Experiment 1 or all 3)
- "What's your first reaction?"
- "What would make you sign up? What would stop you?"
- "How much would you pay for this per month?"
- "What would you cancel or reduce spending on to afford this?"

**Part 5: Demographics + close (5 min)**
- Age, location, household income bracket, current wellness subscriptions
- "Would you be interested in being a founding member?"

### Segment Quotas
| Segment | Target Interviews |
|---|---|
| German women 35-50 | 10 |
| Expat women 30-45 | 8 |
| German women 25-35 | 5 |
| German women 50-60 | 4 |
| Men 30-50 (control) | 3 |
| **Total** | **30** |

## Experiment Sequencing

```
WEEK 1:  Start interviews (Exp 4)
         Commission legal review (Exp 6)
         Design concept boards (Exp 1)

WEEK 2:  Continue interviews
         Concept testing survey launched (Exp 1+2)
         Plan pop-up events

WEEK 3:  Complete concept testing
         Continue interviews
         Event tickets on sale

WEEK 4:  Analyze concept + pricing data
         First pop-up event (Exp 3+5)
         Continue interviews

WEEK 5:  Second pop-up event
         Complete interviews
         Legal review received

WEEK 6:  Third pop-up event
         Synthesize all interview data
         Track conversion from events

WEEK 7:  Fourth pop-up event (optional)
         Compile all validation data
         Draft findings report

WEEK 8:  Final synthesis
         Go/No-Go decision
         Update strategy documents
```

*Every experiment has a kill criterion. Use them. The purpose of validation is not to confirm what you hope -- it's to discover what's true.*
