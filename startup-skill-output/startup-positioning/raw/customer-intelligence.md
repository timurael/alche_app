# Raw Research: Customer Intelligence

> Research date: March 2026. Voice-of-customer data mined from Reddit, Trustpilot, App Store reviews, industry reports, and expert commentary.

---

## Customer Segments Identified

### Primary: The "Overwhelmed Seeker" (Women 30-55)
- Wellness-engaged but burned out on optimization culture
- Spending EUR 50-150/mo across fragmented wellness stack (gym, supplements, occasional nutritionist, apps)
- Wants to age well but rejects "biohacker" identity
- Values science but doesn't want to become a data scientist
- Craves community but current options feel male, competitive, or clinical
- Likely in or approaching perimenopause — hormonal changes creating urgency

**Key insight:** This segment doesn't describe themselves as "longevity enthusiasts." They say "I want to feel good as I age" or "I want energy and clarity" or "I want to take care of myself without it becoming my whole personality." [Estimate]

### Secondary: The "Curious Beginner" (Women 25-35)
- Health-conscious but hasn't entered longevity space yet
- Currently doing: gym, meal prep, maybe an OURA or Apple Watch, follows health accounts on Instagram
- Intimidated by longevity space complexity
- Price-sensitive (EUR 19-49/mo range)
- Attracted to aesthetic, community-forward brands
- Social-media-influenced purchasing decisions

**Key insight:** Gen Z drives 41% of US wellness spend (McKinsey). In Europe, younger cohort may be entry wedge for community/cafe even if they don't immediately convert to full membership. [Data + Estimate]

### Tertiary: The "Disillusioned Optimizer" (Any gender, 30-50)
- Has tried OURA, WHOOP, supplements, protocols
- Experiencing "longevity fixation syndrome" or optimization fatigue
- Ready for a more balanced approach
- Higher willingness to pay but wants quality over quantity
- May include some men who also reject "biohacker bro" culture

## Voice of Customer: Pain Point Clusters

### Cluster 1: Data Without Direction
**Frequency: VERY HIGH across all competitor reviews**

Exact language:
- "Data without implementation" (InsideTracker reviews)
- "Identifies problems but sends you away without a plan"
- "Recommendations are generic lists"
- "Leaving the expo feeling overwhelmed by all my options — and definitely unsure which ones have enough solid science to be worthwhile" (CNN longevity conference reporter)
- "So much promise, too little function" (ZOE review)

**What they want instead:** Curated guidance. "Tell me what to do TODAY, not what my VO2 max is."

### Cluster 2: Optimization as Oppression
**Frequency: HIGH — growing rapidly in 2026**

Exact language:
- "It's easy to feel as though you're doing something wrong if you don't know your VO2-Max"
- "Selling people guilt that they haven't done more already"
- "The relentless drive to optimize every biological function is itself a stress response masquerading as self-care"
- "Sleep is scored, glucose is graphed, aging is tracked, and wellbeing has shifted from something we feel to something we perform correctly"
- "In an era where information is readily accessible, individuals are bombarded with messages promoting the importance of health"
- "Wellness has become another thing to be bad at" (LifeCrunch)

**What they want instead:** Permission to be imperfect. Health as a practice, not a performance.

### Cluster 3: Male-Default Longevity
**Frequency: MEDIUM-HIGH — acute in women's discussions**

Exact language:
- "Biohacking is incredibly male" (Nichol Bradford, longevity investor)
- "The booming longevity market, like medicine decades before it, is tacitly male" (GWS 2026)
- "Most traditional biohacking advice has been designed around male physiology"
- "Not a hack" — women reject the language itself
- "Alpha males wanting to live forever" (Abby Levy, Primetime Partners)
- "Don't call them biohackers" (Fortune headline about women in longevity)

**What they want instead:** Women-centered design. Hormonal awareness. Community that doesn't feel like a tech bro meetup.

### Cluster 4: Subscription Fatigue & Trust Deficit
**Frequency: HIGH — especially in Noom, ZOE, OURA reviews**

Exact language:
- "Another subscription service with no advance notice of recurring charge and no refunds" (Noom review)
- "One of the worst experiences trying to cancel my subscription" (ZOE review)
- "Really shady" — charging membership after product return (OURA review)
- "Stupidly paid for a year" and can't get out (ZOE review)
- Noom settled $62M lawsuit for sneaky subscription practices

**What they want instead:** Transparent pricing. Easy cancellation. Value they can see.

### Cluster 5: Wanting Physical Connection
**Frequency: MEDIUM — growing post-pandemic**

Exact language:
- Cafe/third-space importance "grown by 22%, largely driven by millennials, Gen Z" (food industry data)
- "Places outside of work and home where people can build connections"
- Community-driven retention is #1 lever in fitness (2026 trends)
- "Community as infrastructure, not an afterthought"
- Peloton members who engage socially work out 15% more frequently

**What they want instead:** A place to go. Real people. Shared experience around health.

| Signal | Source | Implication |
|--------|--------|-------------|

| Function Health at $365/yr with 200K+ members | Function data | Annual commitment works if value is clear |

| 72% would prioritize "longevity-boosting" spend over designer accessories | Industry survey | Willingness exists in principle |
| EUR 50-100/mo is most contested band | Existing Alche research | Differentiation must be crystal clear at this price |

**Assessment:** The EUR 19/mo entry tier is well-positioned against ZOE 2.0 ($9.99). The EUR 49/mo mid-tier competes directly with ZOE full ($60/mo) and Levels ($65/mo). The EUR 99/mo premium tier needs to deliver substantially more than competitors at that price — the cafe access + community + biomarkers combination is the justification. [Estimate]

## Community Viability Signals

### What works (proven in adjacent categories):
- Peloton: 88-89% 12-month retention via social features, instructor relationships, leaderboards
- CrossFit: Identity-based community ("I am a CrossFitter")
- Strava: Social sharing of achievements
- Key mechanic: Daily engagement + shareable moments + identity formation

### What's unproven in longevity:
- Biomarker data is private (no one shares "my cortisol is high")
- Engagement is quarterly (blood tests) not daily
- No established identity ("I am a longevity person" doesn't work yet)
- No proven model at scale

### The food hypothesis:
- Food is inherently social and sharable ("look at this beautiful bowl")
- Cafe creates daily/weekly engagement touchpoint
- Meals are photographable (Instagram-native)
- Cooking/eating together is a universal bonding activity
- BUT: completely unvalidated in longevity context [Assumption]

## Key Customer Questions Still Unresolved

1. **Does "Imperfect Longevity" resonate at purchase level?** Stated preference vs. revealed preference gap is the graveyard of consumer brands. People may LOVE the concept but not PAY for it. [Assumption — needs testing]

2. **Is the target segment actually in Berlin?** Berlin skews young, international, price-sensitive. The EUR 99/mo customer may be more common in Munich, Hamburg, or Zurich. [Assumption]

3. **What's the entry behavior?** Does she come for the cafe and discover the app? Or download the app and discover the cafe? The funnel direction determines everything about GTM. [Assumption]

4. **Does "women-first" alienate potential male customers?** Or does it actually attract men who also reject optimization culture? SoulCycle and Peloton both skew female without explicitly saying "for women." [Assumption]

5. **How does the German Heilpraktiker system affect positioning?** This regulatory bridge enables services that are illegal elsewhere, but "Heilpraktiker" carries mixed connotations in Germany — respected by some, dismissed as pseudo-science by others. [Assumption]
